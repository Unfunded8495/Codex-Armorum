## Leaders and Attached Units

Some models, mostly Characters, have either the Leader or Support ability, which allows them to join other units to create a single **Attached** Unit. These operate as a single unit on the Battlefield, usually becoming more than the sum of their parts.

## Leaders and Support

![](rules-deep-dive-special-unit-types-01.jpg)

Cadian Command Squad. Credit: Campbell "SRM" McLaughlin

In 11th Edition, there are **two** abilities that allow a unit to join other units, **Leader** and **Support**. Each works in pretty much the same way - such a unit will specify which units it can lead, and during the Muster Armies step of the battle, you can attach them to those units.

Why are there two different rules that do the same thing? This serves two purposes:

1. It centralises rules that existed in 10th Edition allowing two Characters to join the same unit. Now, each unit can be joined by up to one Leader and up to one Support unit. As an example from the Armageddon box, the mighty Warboss is a Leader, while the Bannernob is Support, so if you want your Waaaagh’s leader *and* a fancy flag in the same unit, you can have that.
1. Support units **must** join a unit during the Muster Armies step; they cannot choose to wander around the table solo. This is a slightly surprising change (and only appears in the extended App rules), and we suspect this is to close off a gap in 10th where Support-style Characters were often among the cheapest things in each Codex, so you saw them taken solo to sit on an objective rather than fulfil their intended buff role.
We won’t know what the full impact of this is till we see which Characters get switched to be Support, but directionally its very likely to broaden the number of double attachment options; even just taking the Datasheets in the Armageddon box, Orks now have vastly more options to do so than in 10th!

## Attachments Happen During Army Construction

Eagle-eyed readers may have spotted another change above - Attaching a Leader and/or Support unit to another unit now happens during Muster Armies, not the pre-game Battle Formations step. This simplifies list building a bit, but for my money is a little bit of a shame; 95% of the time when building a list in 10th the Attachments were fixed, but I quite liked the toolbox flexibility of being able to mix things up. I guess it will make Competitive Innovations easier to write though, because I won’t have to speculate in those 5% edge cases.

## Attachment is Permanent

![](rules-deep-dive-special-unit-types-02.jpg)

Captain Titus and the Wardens of Ultramar. Credit: SRM

In 10th Edition, although Attached Units operated as a single force, it was still possible to destroy separate parts of them. Attached Units are still divided into the Leaders/Support models and **Bodyguard** models, which are those from the units that were joined, but if you run out of Bodyguard Models, or a Leader gets iced via **Precision**, no unit has been Destroyed for rules purposes, and the unit is still an Attached Unit.

This has three main impacts:

- If your opponent needs to destroy a unit to fulfil a mission, they have to take out every last model, and units containing a Bodyguard, Leader and Support will no longer give up three kills for missions that care about the number.
- As long as they are alive, Leader/Support models in an Attached are **always** “leading a unit” for the purpose of abilities they have which care about this. A Librarian no longer forgets how to provide an Invulnerable Save after running out of Bodyguard models (though this effect will stop if the Librarian dies - see below).
- Effects that revive models can put a Bodyguard back together even if every Bodyguard model got wiped out. Be assured that as a Necron player I am *salivating* at the prospect of this.
Taken together with the timing changes, this means that if you’re building a big doomstack for your army, you are fully locked-in to that choice, but the unit you get will hold onto its benefits longer, and is less of a scoring liability in some missions.

## Abilities in Attached Units

One of the big reasons to form Attached units is to let Leaders and Bodyguards benefit from one-another’s abilities. Most Leaders provide a buff to their units, and some units have abilities providing re-rolls that can soup up the damage output of more martial Leaders.

When either part of a unit has an ability, all models in it benefit *as long as* at least one model from the Datasheet that provides the ability is still alive. This means that although an Attached unit is now a permanent fixture, you can still strip away powerful Leader effects by using Precision and the like.

An exception to this is abilities (such as those provided by Wargear) that only affect a specified bearer - these obviously still only affect the specific model, and stop working if that model is destroyed.

***Note:*** *One of the things we’re not totally sure about is how this works with some Core Abilities, such as* ***Feel No Pain*** *and* ***Infiltrators****, where only one Datasheet in the Attached Unit has them. Based on the Attached units rules, it looks like these might propagate automatically, but the wording in the Core Abilities suggests that it still cares about which specific models have the ability. We’re hoping for a clarification on this, as especially for Feel No Pain, it seems unlikely the intention is that every Leader with one can now provide that as an extra unit-wide bonus!*

## Keywords In Attached Units

Different Datasheets that form an Attached unit may have different Keywords. When an attached unit forms, each model still only has the Keywords provided by its own Datasheet, but the Attached Unit has all the Keywords. This can make the whole unit eligible for powerful buffs from Stratagems and Detachment Rules, but also makes them vulnerable to rules like **Anti.**

One thing that’s not totally clear in the rulebook is whether effects that require a certain Keyword affect or can target an Attached unit where every model with that Keyword has been Destroyed. The answer to this is provided in the extended App rules - they **cannot**, so if you want to use a buff for INTERCESSORS on an Attached Unit of five Intercessors and a Captain, at least one Intercessor needs to be alive. For the smart-ass at the back raising their hand to ask if that creates weird issues with timing on **Anti-** weapons; no, they thought ot that, and:

1. **Anti-** is determined at target time.
1. In 11th, models destroyed by attacks are not removed until the attacking unit has resolved all of its attacks.

## Monsters & Vehicles

All armies in Warhammer 40K can bring either mechanical war machines or terrifying monsters (or both) to bear, and these are designated by the VEHICLE and MONSTER keywords. These have some general rules that apply to them.

## Moving Monsters & Vehicles

![Night Spinner](rules-deep-dive-special-unit-types-03.jpg)

Night Spinner. Credit: Wings

Monsters and Vehicles have two differences in how they move around the Battlefield:

- Normally you can move through friendly models, but Monsters/Vehicles cannot move through other models with these Keywords. They’re too big and would smash into each other!
- Normally you *cannot* move through enemy models. When making a Normal or Advance move, Monsters and Vehicles *can* move through enemy models that do not share the Monster/Vehicle keyword.
The first part is the same as in 10th Edition, but the second is new, and makes it harder to fully trap and move-block big models with a line or circle of chaff. One of my least edifying games of 10th Edition involved my Avatar of Khaine being trapped against a wall by three consecutive 5-model units of Drukhari Wyches forming a little ring turn after turn. In 11th, the Avatar could simply walk out and leave.

## Frame

Some Monster/Vehicle models don’t have bases, or protrude considerably beyond them. In this case, they are given the FRAME keyword, which indicates that when placing and measuring distances to the model, all parts of the model are used (the closest, for distance), not just the base. This codifies “measure to hull” into an actual Keyword and rule, which is a big relief all-round.

Frame also explains how you pivot a model without a base - you do so around the central point of the model.

## Towering and Plunging Fire

![Knight Abominant - Norman](rules-deep-dive-special-unit-types-04.jpg)

Knight Abominant - Norman

Some especially large units have the TOWERING keyword, looming large across the Battlefield. In 10th Edition, this modified how they could see and be seen through terrain, but that’s no longer the case in 11th.

Instead, any model with TOWERING that is making ranged attacks against visible foes on ground level that are within 12” automatically benefits from the Plunging Fire rule, getting +1 to their BS. This makes aggressive shooting plays by Imperial and Chaos Knight armies especially terrifying to foes!

## Shooting and Engaged Monsters/Vehicles

Monsters and Vehicles are so large that the enemy can safely shoot at them even when their forces are in Engagement Range. They suffer a -1 penalty to hit while doing so, unless they're engaged with it and shooting Close-Quarters weapons.

## Aircraft

Some factions bring close air support to the battlefield, raking the battlefield with firepower from planes or winged creatures. These have always been a bit awkward, rules-wise, and 11th Edition overhauls them yet again.

## Moving Aircraft

In 11th Edition, Aircraft do not have a Movement Characteristic, and can only ever perform an Ingress Move. At the end of your opponent’s turn, each Aircraft unit you control that’s on the Battlefield must be placed into Strategic Reserves, ready to Ingress again on the following turn. Essentially, Aircraft now make true strafing runs, appearing, shooting, and then vanishing again after the enemy has had a window to fire back or try and smash them from the sky. Aircraft must also start the battle in Strategic Reserves (and do use up your points allocation for reserves), so can only start doing this from the second Battle Round onwards unless they have an ability that lets them do otherwise.

This is, on balance, probably slightly better than their movement situation in 10th, where they still had to make an initial appearance from reserves on turn 2, but were then locked-in to making at least a 20” move straight ahead from that position, often ending up somewhere awkward. They could fly off the board, but then you’d lose a whole turn of shooting with them!

Aircraft also cannot ever make a Charge Move.

## Shooting with Aircraft

![Photo of Biel-tan Crimson Hunter](rules-deep-dive-special-unit-types-05.jpg)

Photo Credit: Musterkrux

Aircraft ignore the Plunging Fire rule in both directions, but otherwise shoot normally. This is a notable change from 10th Edition - Aircraft used to ignore the Obscuring rule both when shooting or being shot at. This change generally makes aircraft a bit less appealing, especially as unless they have Deep Strike, they will always be deploying around the edges of the board, which will give them restricted sight lines on many maps. Bluntly, Aircraft just aren’t very *good* in 10th Edition, and the times they showed up were usually as tech pieces on specific terrain packs (e.g. UKTC) where being able to shoot into specific staging spots through terrain was high value. Without that, they’ll need to be pretty cheap to be worth taking.

## Interacting with Aircraft

Because an Aircraft’s position on the battlefield is a fairly abstract representation of the plane zipping past, they have limited battlefield impact on units without FLY. Enemy models can always move through Aircraft, and enemy models without FLY can largely ignore them for the purposes of rules that require picking and moving towards nearby enemy units (e.g. Surge Moves). Aircraft do still have an Engagement Range that models cannot normally end moves within.

Only models with FLY can interact with Aircraft in the Charge/Fight Phases as well. Only Units with FLY can select Aircraft as Charge Targets, and only they can make melee attacks at Aircraft.

This does plausibly create some slightly weird interactions against non-FLY units, as the Engagement Range around an Aircraft can be huge, and potentially impact on nearby enemies being able to successfully charge targets sheltering near them. This probably only ever arises for Aircraft that can Deep Strike, as they’re the only ones that will end up in places where precision melee placement will really matter, but I suspect at some point the restrictions here will change to let non-FLY units end a Charge Move within engagement of an Aircraft, even if they didn’t select it as a Charge Target.
