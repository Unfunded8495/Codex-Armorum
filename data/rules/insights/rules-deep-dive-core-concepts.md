This piece is aimed primarily at existing players, and we’re going to focus on what’s changed rather than digging into the full mechanics of the phase. If you’re a brand new player looking to dive into 11th Edition, we recommend you go and take a look at our main review, as that provides a full outline of how Warhammer 40,000 works on the tabletop!

As a note - for our review, we have been provided with access to the extended digital rules that will be made generally available in the Warhammer 40,000 app. This means that in a few places, we may talk about a rule or specific nuance that hasn’t been covered in previews or things you’ve seen elsewhere. We’ll try to flag the important ones!

## What’s the Same

We’ll start with what hasn’t changed since 10th Ed, and the answer is plenty. All of the key building blocks of 10th Edition Warhammer 40,000 are still here. Even if they’ve changed a bit, concepts like Battleshock, Invulnerable Saves, Coherency and Engagement remain, and the set of phases that make up a turn is identical.

The Core Concepts section of the rulebook also takes you through definitions of key concepts like Armies, units, and models, none of which have functionally changed, plus how you measure distances and roll dice.

You’ll find a similar pattern as we go through all the phases and elements of the game - almost everything you’re used to is here in some shape or form, but plenty of it has changed, and 11th introduces some whole new mechanics like Hidden that will add new dynamics to the game.

## What’s Changed in Core Concepts?

![](rules-deep-dive-core-concepts-01.png)

Credit: Robert "TheChirurgeon" Jones

There’s quite a few meaningful changes here that will have some major impacts on how you play the game. To start, we can talk about how the rules themselves are laid out. Rules are now broken out into blocks, each with a number designation which makes referencing said rules much easier. This was implemented in the new edition of AoS with a lot of success so it's no surprise to see it here.

The downside is there are quite a few rules that are only in the app. While I’m someone whose core book lives on their shelf the nanosecond after it gets home, having rules just not be complete feels a bit off and it's somewhat odd not to just have a digital ruleset all together if you’re gonna go this route. There’re these little tabs on the page that’ll tell you to go download the 40k app for more info on various topics, which sure does feel like they could have just put that stuff on the page.

***Wings:*** *I think the kinder reading on this is that quite a few of the rules that are app only are things where they want more freedom to tune or iterate the exact wording, or make functional changes over time. I’d agree there are a few strange absences however, perhaps the most glaring being that the rules for Heal, a rules term used without in-line explanation in some of the previewed stratagems, don’t appear in the rulebook.*

From here, we have a bit of a scattershot of topics, so we’ll just address them one at a time.

## Rules Presentation

Various different groups of rules (e.g. types of move, Actions, types of shooting, Stratagems) have each been given a standard template that they consistently follow. These make the process and conditions for the rules very clear, and help ensure that identical verbiage is used each time. Some of these rules are also “modal” - you will be provided with multiple options to choose between at the start, and then have different instructions to follow as you proceed through them. An example of this is Disembarking - this is a type of move, and uses the template for this, but within it there are three different modes, covering the two types of Disembark you could do in 10th Edition (before or after moving), and a new one that’s been added in 11th.

## Active and Opposing Player and Sequencing

![](rules-deep-dive-core-concepts-02.jpg)

Cobalt Scions Terminators face Goff Meganobz during a boarding action. Credit: Charlie Brassley

This is a concept that's existed for a bit that’s a bit more codified now. If it’s your turn, you’re considered the “Active Player” which generally means you get to choose how certain interactions stack up if things trigger at the same time. Now though, if you’re in the middle of a move on your opponent’s turn or selected to shoot or fight you become the active player until those actions have resolved.

The digital rules also outline how the active/opposing players sequence rules that have identical timing (e.g. "at the end of the movement phase").

First, the active player does all their mandatory abilities, then selects and activates all their optional abilities. After that, the Opposing Player does the same. If any *new* rules are triggered by things either player does, those do not happen until all pre-existing rules have resolved.

This cleans up some of the more nebulous parts of Warhammer 40K, and also takes away an issue 10th had, which was that the Active Player always chose the sequencing of rules, and thus could sometimes neutralise potentially powerful combos simply by declaring the order they wanted. Now, the opposing player usually has the last say on any given trigger, letting them respond effectively.

## Invulnerable Saves

Invulnerable saves are largely unchanged. They’re still an unmodifiable save that you use in place of your normal save. The big change though is that they are no longer optional. There were times last edition where it was advantageous to use your invulnerable or normal save in order to better force a unit of yours to die. Now you check the value of the roll first against your invulnerable save and then your normal save to see if damage is inflicted.

## Turn Structure

Turns and phases now have a defined Start and End step along with a defined scoring sub-step. Functionally, this won’t change anything for your games, but things can now get much more specific with timing to fix race conditions. This may get more involved once new books start coming out and rules reference these specific sub-steps.

## Hazard Rolls

![](rules-deep-dive-core-concepts-03.jpg)

Black Templars Hellblasters. Credit: SRM

Hazard rolls are a new core concept that will be familiar to those who used Hazardous weapons last edition. Hazard rolls are forced by certain rules like weapons with the HAZARDOUS tag, desperate escapes and emergency disembarkation from transports. The effect is simple, you roll a D6 and on a 1-2 you take a mortal wound or 3 if you’re a Monster or Vehicle. This means rules that interact with Hazard rolls now have a lot more breadth of impact than just your plasma getting hot, as well as making your desperate escapes slightly less desperate (though those now have other drawbacks).

## Actions Are Back in the Core Rules

Thank *god*. There was some weirdness in the last edition with actions never being properly codified in the core rules which meant that it had to be reprinted with every new Chapter Approved release. Actions get a new structure too. Each Action gets a defined Start, Unit field (who can do it), Use Limit, Completes (when it's done) and Effect field, making them slightly easier to read to find the relevant information you’re looking for.

To perform an Action, a unit must:

- Have at least OC1
- Be on the Battlefield
- Not be an Aircraft/Fortification
- Not have Advanced/Fallen Back this turn.
- Not have started another Action this turn.
In addition, all types of shooting prevent a unit being eligible to start an Action for the rest of the turn, and the reverse is also true; If you start an action, then for the rest of the turn, you are not eligible to Shoot, and are also not eligible to Charge.

Titanic models can all do actions when engaged and can shoot and do actions but otherwise they’re the same as 10th’s last iteration of them.

## Battle-shock

Battle-shock is also ever so slightly different! You still roll 2d6 and check to see if it's below your leadership, but now on a failure your OC becomes “-” instead of 0. The null value means it can’t be modified after the fact, which was the case before. You also check to see if you need to make such a check at Half-Strength, meaning 2 model units are no longer immune. Lastly, and this is likely the biggest change, you must take another Battle-shock test in your next command phase to see if you become un-Battle-shocked. This means abilities that force a test in your turn gain a ton of value, since it means there's another chance for the unit to stay Battle-shocked before scoring.

## Army Construction

![](rules-deep-dive-core-concepts-04.jpg)

Cobalt Scions Space Marines army. Credit: Charlie Brassley

Army construction features in the extended app rules only, and has some changes from 10th Edition. Much of it will be familiar - you still select an Army Faction, points limits are the same, you still have a limit of three of each Datasheet (except Dedicated Transports and Battleline, which are doubled). However, there's some new things as well:

- Rather than picking a single Detachment, you now have a pool of Detachment Points to spend, allowing you to take more than one. You get three of these at 2000pts.
- Joining Characters to units happens during army construction, not before the battle, so you cannot flex your Leaders between different units.
- Some Leaders have now become **Support** Characters instead. These work the same way as Leaders, but *must* be joined to a unit, so cannot be taken independently. You can attack one Leader and one Support Character to a single unit, so this centralises "double attachment" rules.
- Picking Enhancements for your units happens after attaching Leaders/Support, and each unit can only take one, which means you cannot combine two Enhancements in the same squad.
- Some Enhancements are now **Upgrades**. These can be taken on non-Character units, and each Upgrade can be taken up to three times while only using a single Enhancement pick, though you do still have to pay for each. As far as we can tell, they do still count towards the one Enhancement per unit limit, so you cannot combine them with something on a Leader.
- Your Warlord must now have the same Faction keyword as your Army Faction. This mostly affects some occasional Imperium armies that would use an allied Inquisitor Draxus as their Warlord.

## Modifiers

The digital rules includes details of how modifiers apply, and what limit you can modify different Characteristics to, much of which is going to be familiar to existing players. However, everyone's favourite rule, the one for applying multiple modifiers (most commonly to damage), now gets rewritten, and will hopefully make things play more naturally.

When applying multiple modifiers to a characteristic, you do so in this order:

- Apply anything that sets a specific value.
  - If you set a value to 0 or - at this step, stop and do not process any further modifiers.
- Apply multiplication
- Apply addition
- Apply division
- Apply subtraction
- Round any fractions up
For those not au-fait with the nuances of intricate rules interactions from previous editions, this has three common impacts:

1. Effects that set damage to 0 no longer get partially bypassed by +D effects. Here they now do nothing.
1. Effects that half damage no longer get bypassed by Melta. You now add the damage, then halve it. This also removes the weird edge case that against a weapon doing 1+1 damage, you were better off with a -1D effect than a half damage effect.
1. Buffs that provide +1OC no longer bypass Battle-shock, both because it now sets that value to "-", and even if it *did* still set the value to 0, you'd ignore further modifiers.
There are also modification limits for the new **Detection Distance** mechanic of Hidden, and also on Lone Operative range (Lone Operative can be be Lone Operative X"). Effects cannot result these being to be better than 9" or worse than 30". This is a nerf for the small amount of what are functionally Lone Operative 6" effects out there.
