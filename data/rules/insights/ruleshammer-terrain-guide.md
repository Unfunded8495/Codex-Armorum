*Welcome back to Ruleshammer! In this series we take a detailed look at the rules of key games, but Warhammer 40k in particular. In our first 11th edition article, we're looking at terrain rules in the new edition.*

This article primarily deals with terrain as a feature of the board and how it interacts with models' ability to shoot, be shot at, and move. If you’re interested in reading more about how terrain interacts with missions as an objective, please read our [review of that concept here](#rules-deep-dive-terrain-and-objectives). This guide is not a replacement for having read the rules, but should help with a few edge cases that aren’t clear; where necessary I’ve included quotes and references to the rules (such as their new handy rules numbers) to help.

## Terrain Areas 13.01

During the placing terrain step of preparing a battlefield for Warhammer 40k 11th edition both players need to agree how each **terrain area** will be defined. The rules give a few different methods for doing this which the diagram below explains in a bit more detail.

![](ruleshammer-terrain-guide-01.png)

Practically I’d expect **terrain area** mats to be the predominant method, the areas of specific sizes for the new mission layouts are being sold by GW and are well documented. When using a GW mission layout some touching areas are joined into one larger area, and some are considered separate.

![](ruleshammer-terrain-guide-02.png)

Be aware that every piece of terrain creates a **terrain area** in 11th.

> In each case, the area of the battlefield occupied by that boundary or **terrain feature** is known as a **terrain area**. A mission’s deployment map may define the location and dimensions of each **terrain area**. Otherwise, players must agree on this before the battle.

Why this matters I’ll highlight later on in the guide, but it’s important to note that **terrain features** that have previously rarely had an area do create one and probably need that area to be shown in some way on your battlefields. Features such as barricades and pipes have had a multitude of rules in the past about how close you need to be to benefit from them, these rules are not present in 11th edition.

## Terrain Categories 13.02

Unlike previous editions where one “piece” of terrain had a singular type, **terrain features** within an **terrain area** in 11th edition have individually assigned **categories** of their own. One larger wall might be **Dense terrain**, whilst a smaller “chest high” (if not Votann or some lesser Tyranids) walls might be **Light Terrain**. The three types are:

- **Exposed**. Craters, Razorwize, Debris and similar.
- **Light**. Barricades, Low Walls, and Statues.
- **Dense**. Buildings, Ruins, Armoured Containers, and woods.
None of these lists are exhaustive though, so if something on the board doesn’t immediately fall into one of the **categories** make sure you and your opponent agree on what type it is. This will be important later.

## Terrain and Movement 13.06

Movement depends on the keywords of the model you’re moving and the **category** of the **terrain feature** they’re trying to move through.

**Exposed** and **Light** terrain does not block movement at all for any models.

> All models can move horizontally and vertically through **exposed** and **light terrain features.**

![](ruleshammer-terrain-guide-03.png)

**Dense terrain** has a few different ways models interact with it, again based on keywords of the model moving. Some aspects of this are quite different to previous editions.

***INFANTRY/BEAST/SWARM/MOBILE*** *can move horizontally through* ***dense terrain features****.*

On the face of it this seems very similar to previous editions but when you consider that dense terrain features are not just walls, but also things like Armoured Containers, or some of the industrial terrain you can buy it’s potentially a pretty significant change.

![](ruleshammer-terrain-guide-04.png)

***INFANTRY/BEAST/SWARM*** *models can move vertically through* ***dense terrain features****.*

This rule essentially means models with those keywords can climb ruins and terrain as you’d expect. One element of this that has changed though is that models now need to get within a ½” of any terrain they wish to climb. Fundamentally this just codifies what I think a lot of players already presumed, that you had to actually be near a terrain piece to “climb” it."

![](ruleshammer-terrain-guide-05.png)

Models without the **INFANTRY/BEAST/SWARM** keywords can’t move through the floors but if they have the **FLY** or **MONSTER** keywords they can end a move on upper floors if they meet certain conditions

> After ending that move, that model is stable and no part of its base overhangs the outer edge of that surface.

Also take note that **FLY** models don’t need to fly around floors like they have done in older editions if they “take to the skies”.

> Ignore all vertical distance for the purposes of how far it has moved. It can move through all types of model (including enemy models and **MONSTER/VEHICLE** models). It can move horizontally and vertically through all **categories** of **terrain feature**.

For a -2” movement penalty FLY models can move through terrain features like **INFANTRY/BEAST/SWARM** models can.

![](ruleshammer-terrain-guide-06.png)

None **INFANTRY/BEAST/SWARM/FLY/MONSTER** models can climb over terrain but cannot end a move above ground level, remember though that this is only necessary if the terrain feature is more than 2” tall. Anything less than 2" tall can be ignored when moving a model, or less than 4" if you have the super heavy walker rule like Imperial Knights.

## Terrain and Visibility 13.07

Terrain plays a vital role in Warhammer 40k games and 11th edition iterates on the rules we’re used to in a few key ways, some of them pretty significant changes from previous editions. Arguably this edition represents the biggest change to terrain fundamentals since the obscuring concept was added a few editions ago. Cover Saves which have been a part of 40k in some form since 3rd edition in 1998 are gone, though cover is still very much around! *Any 2nd edition players who were around back then and adamant that “cover hit modifiers were fine” in 2nd edition and that “changing it was unnecessary,” errr, congrats I guess?*

## Benefit of Cover 13.08

11th edition streamlines cover significantly. It doesn’t vary as much between **terrain categories** as much as it did in 10th. It’s no longer slightly different for ruins, woods, etc. Rather than dwelling on how it worked in the past (which can all now forget) let me explain how it works now!

Key to the new cover mechanic is that it’s a benefit conveyed to units that meet certain conditions, not models!

> Each time a ranged attack targets a unit, if every model in that unit meets one or more of the following conditions, that unit has the benefit of cover against that attack:

The first condition applies to specific keyworded units only, but that doesn’t mean units lacking those keywords are excluded from cover, they just need to meet the second criteria.

> That model has the **INFANTRY/BEAST/SWARM** keyword and is within a terrain area.

![](ruleshammer-terrain-guide-07.png)

Note that it’s only within! Not “wholly within”, so “toeing into cover” is possible in 11th.

## Vehicles in Cover

> That model is **not fully visible** to the attacking model due to one or more intervening **terrain features** and/or one or more intervening **obscuring terrain areas**.

The first part of this rule is where units without the **INFANTRY/BEAST/SWARM** keywords can gain cover, they don’t benefit automatically for being within the area. For these units you need to check line of sight and visibility physically.

![](ruleshammer-terrain-guide-08.png)

Almost all terrain you’ll encounter at events and I’d wager most FLGS will be Light or Dense terrain; Pipes, Barricades, Statues, Containers, Ruin Walls and the like. As such they will all be obscuring terrain areas, something I’ll elaborate on further in the next section of this article; the bit that matters here is that if you’re on the other side of an obscuring terrain area you’re out of sight, because it’s obscuring. There is however a type of terrain feature that won’t block Line of Sight that models can gain the benefit of cover from: Exposed terrain features! That’s right, if you find a table somewhere with some razor wire it does provide cover!

What about the “obscuring terrain areas” though?

## Obscuring 13.09

Back as a full rule in its own right, obscuring now applies to any **terrain area** that has one or more **light** or **dense terrain features**. It’s not something players need to decide on per **terrain area** as they’ve been required to do in the past. Some **terrain categories** that weren’t line of sight blocking in previous editions such as woods can block line of sight in 11th. Note that while woods are not featured on the GW mission layouts you can replace any dense terrain feature with a tree in this edition without any significant change to how that **terrain area** functions.

## Obscuring and Cover

One of the subtle changes between the obscuring we’ve seen in the past is the way **obscuring terrain** areas can convey the benefit of cover, even if no **terrain features** actually cause the model partially behind the **terrain area** to be **not fully visible**.

![](ruleshammer-terrain-guide-09.png)

## What if two units are partially within the same terrain area?

Units attacking accross the same terrain area can still gain cover in 11th edition, but cover and and obscuring are per model checks! You need to consider what is visible for each attacking model to determine if the cover penalty applies. Here's an example.

![](ruleshammer-terrain-guide-10.png)

## Obscuring and Line of Sight

> If every line of sight drawn between two models crosses one or more **obscuring terrain areas** (excluding **obscuring terrain areas** that one or both of those models are within), those two models are **not visible** to each other.

![](ruleshammer-terrain-guide-11.png)

Remember that the obscuring rule doesn’t apply if both units are within the same obscuring terrain area. Yes, obscuring does still block line of sight between terrain areas in ways that can seem unintuitive, such as blocking line of sight between units both on the top floor of different terrain areas with an area of lower obscuring terrain between them. All editions of Warhammer 40k require a level of abstraction to make the game work well as a game. If you want to imagine the buildings are on fire, or that artillery bombardment has created a dust cloud limiting your units line of sight, please do!

## What about Towering and Aircraft?

In 11th edition the TOWERING and AIRCRAFT rules have no impact on how obscuring functions. Every unit can see normally through obscuring terrain areas if within (not wholly within) the terrain area, and AIRCRAFT can be blocked from line of sight by it like all other models.

## Solid 13.11

This is a new rule for 11th edition, but is also really just GW codifying a very common tournament house rule. It does two things but they’re frustratingly covered in two different parts of the core rules. The main rule itself is about Line of Sight.

> Dense terrain features have the **Solid** rule. Line of sight cannot be drawn across any enclosed gap in the surface of such a **terrain feature** that is 3" or less from ground level.

![](ruleshammer-terrain-guide-13.png)

The other element of this rule though is as much about movement as it is about Line of Sight; found on 48 nestled into the sidebar Solid has this additional effect:

> If a terrain feature has the **Solid** rule (13.11), as **dense terrain features** do, a model cannot end a move such that any part of it is through any enclosed part of that **terrain feature** that is 3" or less from ground level – not even through small openings such as doors and windows. This ensures that protruding elements of models cannot be used to circumvent the visibility restrictions of the Solid rule.

So you can’t stick a gun barrel through a window or door in 11th edition and gain line of sight through the wall that way! It also means you explicitly can’t end a move with a model part way through these features, even if the doors and gaps might allow you to.
