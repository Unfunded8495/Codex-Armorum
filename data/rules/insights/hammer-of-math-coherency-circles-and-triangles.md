# Hammer of Math: Coherency, Circles, and Triangles
Series: Hammer of Math
Slug: hammer-of-math-coherency-circles-and-triangles
Order: 51
Source-Doc: Hammer of Math 11th Edition Coherency, Circles, and Triangles.docx

*In Hammer of Math, we look at various statistical concepts in Warhammer 40k, looking at how to evaluate different options mathematically and understanding how those probabilities and statistics affect your games. In this article we're looking at coherency rules in 11th edition and some unique geometric quirks which can help you in ways you might not have expected.*

Welcome back to another 11th edition installment of Hammer of Math. [**Last Time Around**](#hammer-of-math-the-benefit-of-cover) we talked about the new rules for cover in 11th edition, and how those might affect your weapon selection, seeing that higher volumes of low AP attacks can yield better results. This time around we're talking about the new coherency rules and secant lines. For those of you who need the refresher from high school geometry, a secant line is a straight line that passes through a circle and intersects it at exactly two distinct points.

The big change the new coherency rules established in 11th edition was a maximum range for your models with regard to Coherency. In tenth edition if you had more than six models in your unit, every model had to be within 2" of at least two other models. This meant that you could string units out across incredibly long distances, so long as you put a triangle-shaped configuration of models on each end of the line to maintain that 2" of two models requirement.

11th edition makes a key change to this by removing the "two models" requirement but making it so that no model in the unit can be more than 9" from any other model. This effectively puts a maximum radius on how much space units can occupy, though the changes to "within" this edition - specifically removing most instances of "wholly within" means you can set a model on the outer edge of the ring and get interesting with your spacing. In the example below, I've stretched out a unit of 25mm bases, representing the maximum area you can block off with a unit of ten Poxwalkers.

![](hammer-of-math-coherency-circles-and-triangles-01.jpg)

This gives you around 10.8" of total width for blocking, though remember that in 11th edition models can move through your engagement range, they just can't end inside it. Interestingly this means you can now block of wider areas with small units on 40mm bases, since they can overhang the edge of your coherency circle by a larger amount, and filling a 9" across gap with 2" spacing can be done with three models.

![](hammer-of-math-coherency-circles-and-triangles-02.jpg)

Spread across this way you can max out around 12" in width for your area blocking, and things can get even crazier when you consider things like 80mm oval bases for units like bikes - Outriders can do some silly things with the new coherency rules.

Of course, in the rush to create new game aids, we've seen a lot of 9" circular tools repurposed for tracking unit coherency. These are powerful tools, and convenient to use, but they don't account for all of the ways you can set up in coherency. There's a fun edge case using two secant lines where you can set your units up and get a wider coverage area:

![](hammer-of-math-coherency-circles-and-triangles-03.jpg)

In this special case, the three corner units of our equilateral triangle are all within 9" of each other. Note that this won't work for polygons with more sides; a 9" square will have a diagonal of 12.72".

So where is this helpful? Well the reason you go through all of these thought exercises around is because you need to screen units out in the Movement phase. If you can stop a unit from touching an objective in the Movement phase, then you can prevent them from performing actions on that objective while forcing them to charge you in the Charge phase in order to be able to actually capture that objective. If they merely shoot your unit to death, they won't be on the objective, and won't score for holding it, either at the end of their turn or the start of their next Command phase.

The change to 2" Engagement Ranges makes this much easier, though with the caveat that smaller models can walk through those ranges and the little bump-outs on 11th edition terrain can make this more of a chore.

![](hammer-of-math-coherency-circles-and-triangles-04.jpg)

In this example we have a coherent unit of six models on 40mm bases screening out an entire large area terrain feature. When you are doing this the corners need special attention - you have to be closer to the sides in order to avoid leaving room for a unit to "toe in" on the corner of the terrain feature. Going back to our note about the diagonal of a square being longer than the sides, if you place 2" in from each side, you'll be about 3.17" in from the corner.

![](hammer-of-math-coherency-circles-and-triangles-05.jpg)

The name of the game here is using space effectively. Having a 2" Engagement Range means more units can fight but it also makes screening enemy units out way easier and way more important, provided you can stop them from ending up on the other side of you - which is easy to do with ruins and area terrain where any gap less than 3" wide won't fit a model, even on a 25mm base.

That said, the one thing to remember here is that your opponent can always just charge you, either using the **Heroic Intervention** core stratagem or just a regular charge in the Charge phase so presumably if you're doing this, it's with the idea that you'll win that particular combat with your superior, 40mm-based units and put them in an even worse position.
