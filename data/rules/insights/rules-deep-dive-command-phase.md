## The Command Phase

![](rules-deep-dive-command-phase-01.jpg)

Credit: PierreTheMime

Each player’s turn begins with a Command Phase, where players stock up on resources, activate special abilities, and check whether the morale of their injured units holds up to the punishment they’ve taken. A few things have changed here, most notably that morale is now a bit more fragile, and more likely to have a major impact on your games.

## Start and End of Phase

Since this is the first phase we’re covering, it’s the first places you’ll notice that all phases now have an explicit “Start of Phase” and “End of Phase” step, where abilities that trigger or are activated at this point occur. Both of these are governed by the new sequencing rules, so in each of these sub-steps, the active player (whose turn it is) resolves all mandatory rules (in the order they choose), then all their optional abilities, after which the opposing player does the same. This means that the opposing player always gets the “final say” on activating optional effects in any phase. That’s more relevant for phases like Movement (where there’s now no window for the active player to do anything after a Rapid Ingress), but worth bringing up here.

## Mission Rules at the End of the Phase

The End of the Command Phase step has a sub-step for scoring missions, which always happens *after* all other End of the Phase effects are processed. This means that scoring is always evaluated based on the final state after all the dust of special abilities has settled.

## Command Abilities Step

Everything in the Command Phase that doesn’t happen either at the start or end of the phase, or in the Battle-shock step (which we’ll talk about in a moment) now happens in a dedicated step. Importantly, this is **after** Battle-shock, so any effects that happen in the Command Phase that return models to units will now happen after evaluating whether the unit needs to test its morale. This weakens the resistance of units with reliable access to re-spawn effects (and indeed if they’re Stratagem-based and you fail Battle-shock, then you’re totally out of luck).

## Battle-shock

![Necron Psychomancer](rules-deep-dive-command-phase-02.jpg)

Necron Psychomancer. Credit: Pendulin

Right, Battle-shock. This is the biggest change in the Command Phase, because getting Battle-shocked in 11th Edition is both easier and worse than in 10th Edition. In the Battle-shock step of the Command Phase, any unit that is at or below Half-Strength has to take a test against its Leadership, and if it fails it is Battle-shocked. Battle-shocked units have their Objective Control characteristic set to a value of “-” (an unmodifiable zero), can’t be targeted with Stratagems, and cannot perform any Actions (and fail any that were ongoing).

The first two changes are there in that paragraph - you now take Battle-shock if you are **at** Half-Strength, not just below it, which removes the oddity in 10th Edition that units which started with two models in them would never have to test (and makes such tests much more common for 4W solo models, the other type of unit that almost never cared). The other is that rather than setting your OC to 0 (which could then be modified back up with something like an Ancient’s banner), the stat goes to “-”, so there’s nothing you can do to salvage things.

The other new threat here is that, as well as units that are at or below Half-Strength, you also have to test for any units that started the Phase Battle-shocked. No more automatic recovery - you now have to get it together with a passed Leadership test, and with almost all Ld values being between 6 and 8, this is *never* a sure thing. In addition - the **Insane Bravery** Stratagem is still available to automatically pass a Battle-shock test once per game, but *you can’t use that on a unit that’s already Battle-shocked*, so anything trying to recover is rolling the dice.

![](rules-deep-dive-command-phase-03.jpg)

Primaris Chapter Master Dante. Credit: Jack Hunter

This immediately ups the value of every single effect in the game that can inflict Battle-shock on enemy units, and makes a few models like Commander Dante who do so in a radius complete nightmares; they can potentially shatter an army’s cohesion for a prolonged period all by themselves. The converse, units that provide the ability to remove Battle-shock like a Royal Warden, also shoot up in priority when building lists. In addition, this is a major nerf to Transports, because being forced to make an Emergency Disembark still automatically Battle-shocks a unit, and they might not recover to hold whatever objective they landed on now!

Expect Battle-shock to have far more impact on your games, and stock up on tokens for tracking it.
