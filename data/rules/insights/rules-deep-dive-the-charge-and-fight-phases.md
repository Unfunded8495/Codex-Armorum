## The Charge Phase

How a unit Charges has changed significantly in 11th Edition, now being much simpler and a bit more abstract in how it’s resolved.

The Charge Phase has a start and end of phase step like all other phases in 11th Edition.

## Charging in 11th Edition

![](rules-deep-dive-the-charge-and-fight-phases-01.jpg)

Credit: Evan "Felime" Siefring

The big difference for Charging in 11th Edition is that the success or failure of a Charge Roll is now determined solely by the distance between the two units. In your Charge phase, any number of your eligible units (by default, all your units that are unengaged, within 12” of one or more enemies, and didn’t Advance or Fall-Back this turn) can attempt a charge.

For each that does, you roll 2d6, and that gives you a **maximum distance** in inches. You can then pick one or more enemy units that are within the maximum distance of your unit to be your **charge targets** and attempt to execute a Charge Move to engage them.

When making a Charge Move, you move every model in your unit up to the maximum distance that you rolled, and at the end of that you must be engaged with all the charge targets you selected. In addition, each model must end the move closer to one of your charge targets, and follow the following additional constraints (in descending order of priority):

- They must end within 1” of one of the charge targets if possible.
- They must end within engagement range of one of the charge targets if possible.
You also cannot end the move engaged with any enemy units you did not pick as a charge target.

If you cannot achieve that with the movement you have (e.g. because you need to go the long way round a corner), then you cannot move. Units that successfully complete a Charge Move can **Fight First** in the following Fight Phase.

*Lots* of changes there, so let’s highlight them one at a time:

1. You no longer have to pick your targets before making a Charge Roll - you can toss the dice and see what you have to work with. This takes a bit of risk/reward calculations of the phase away, which is a shame, but speeds things up a bit.
1. Your required distance to *try* and make a charge move is solely determined point-to-point between the two units, and with 2” Engagement Range, if you succeed on the roll you essentially have 2” of slack to work with, so will often succeed. It also means that without a bonus to your charge, a double 1 is an automatic failure, as you can never start a charge within 2” of the foe.
1. Charges out of Deep Strike/Strategic Reserves still need a 9 on the roll. The set up distance for these is now >8”, so you can never be within 8” of the target when setting up. This also makes the rare effects that allow reduced setup distance that don’t prevent charges (e.g. Deathshroud Terminators) slightly worse (unless they also get changed) because the charges are essentially 1” harder.
1. Staying an inch back from a wall no longer protects you from being charged by Monsters etc. With 2” Engagement range, they can swing a *long* way through obstacles.
1. You don’t have to base your enemy, only get within 1”. This can be used to tee up some clever pile-in moves in the subsequent Fight Phase, helping you engage or trap the enemy.
1. It’s harder to string models back in mid-sized units. Engagement range is so big that it’s much harder to block out your own models from it compared to mandatory basing in 10th Edition. You can choose to leave an individual model stationary as long as your other models can meet the criteria, but that’s a lot less flexibility than you often had in 10th.
1. If you roll a charge, and you don’t fancy any of the targets you can reach, you can choose not to move. There’s no obligation to execute a charge after trying one.
The general trend here is for Charges to be smoother and a bit easier, and you’re going to get more big swingy high rolls happening simply because you no longer have to pre-select between an easy 5” and a potentially game-changing 11” option.

## No Overwatch

The other thing that will speed up the Charge Phase is that there is no Overwatch in it. That’s in the movement phase only now, so make sure you get your defensive shooting in then.

## Heroic Intervention

The **Heroic Intervention** Stratagem now happens at the end of the phase, and has been considerably buffed in 11th Edition. The standard 1CP version of this still only lets you counter-charge enemy units that have charged, but no longer has a 6” cap on it, so you can go for a big reach if it’s your only hope. You also resolve the charge same as above, so if multiple enemies have ended charges within range, you can tag both of them, or roll the dice to see if you hit a stretch goal, and select a consolation target if not.

There’s also now a 2CP version available to all armies that allows them to charge *any* units, but does cap their charge distance at 6”. This means you can no longer safely score by running up to a high threat melee unit and standing innocently on an objective in front of them, at least not if the opponent has space Command Points.

## The Fight Phase

The Fight Phase gets the biggest overhaul of any part of the turn in 11th Edition, shaking up both when and how units move, and who can fight. For those who felt combat movement got a bit too constrained in 10th Edition it’s good news - you have quite a bit more flexibility in how you position your units, and also some opportunities to create massive power plays with careful sequencing of your fights.

The Fight Phase has a start and end of phase step like all other phases in 11th Edition.

## Pile-In

![](rules-deep-dive-the-charge-and-fight-phases-02.jpg)

Cthonian Beserks. Credit: Rich Nutter

The first big change in this phase is that all units now Pile In in a shared step as the first piece of action once start of phase rules are complete. Each unit that is either engaged or that made a charge move this turn is eligible to pile in. You start with the Active Player, who gets to make a pile-in move with all of their eligible units, after which the Opposing Player makes all of their pile-ins (but may find themselves constrained with what they can achieve).

A Pile-In move uses the rules template for a type of move, and lets each model in the unit move up to 3”, with some restrictions. Before moving, you select your **Pile-In Targets**, and when making a Pile-In, each model must end its move closer to and (if possible) engaged with the closest Pile-In Target. Which units you can select as targets varies depending on your current situation. If you are already engaged, you can *only* (and must) select all units you are currently engaged with as your targets. This can somewhat constrain your ability to slingshot into a unit you did not Charge, because you cannot select them as a target. With 2” engagement range, there’s still plenty of room to force foes into the fight, but you can’t just push off in a completely different direction.

If you are not currently engaged (rare in this step, but important shortly, stay tuned), you can instead select one or more units within 5” to be your Pile-In targets, and then move as above. Importantly, in this case there’s no longer a requirement that you *must* go for the closest target, so you can concentrate your efforts on a single target rather than getting pulled in different directions.

As with 10th Edition, models that are already in base contact with the enemy cannot make a Pile-In move. This means that although there’s no *obligation* to base foes with this any more, the Active Player will often want to do so when making their Pile-In moves in order to restrict their opponents options.

## Eligibility to Fight

Once all units have Piled-In, we move to the Fight Step, and need to determine which units can do so. All units that Charged are eligible to Fight, as are any that are engaged, and any that **start** this step stay eligible even if they later become unengaged. The latter is important; in previous editions, a unit that did not Charge could get pulled out of engagement as their foes suffered casualties, and not end up getting to Fight. Now, doing that doesn’t stop them getting their chance to contribute, and when they activate they will get to make an **Overrun Fight**, which gives them an additional Pile-In Move to get back into combat.

That doesn’t mean you cannot game the threat the foe poses by careful casualty allocation. Because units don’t get additional Pile-Ins by default, the trick now is to try and leave *one* model alive, just within 2” of a single model in the enemy squad, at which point they won’t get to Pile-In, and only that single model will swing. Doesn’t help if it’s a Primarch or something, but it can be a lifesaver against a large unit that kills by volume.

Which models in a unit are eligible to make attacks with their weapons is also different in 11th Edition. This is now solely determined by the 2” engagement distance - if you’re within that, you can swing, if you aren’t, tough luck. This still lets multiple ranks of models in a large unit fight, as 2” is a reasonably big distance, but with units that have really large bases, you can’t base one enemy model with one base, then have the rest of the unit fight through that.

What happens if you’re eligible to Fight, but aren’t engaged with any enemies? As mentioned above, this is handled by **Overrun Fights**.

## Overrun Fight

![](rules-deep-dive-the-charge-and-fight-phases-03.jpg)

Slaves to Darkness Daemon Prince. Credit: SRM

When a unit is selected to Fight, if they are either not currently Engaged *or* if they started the Fight Step unengaged, and are now engaged (usually because another unit has done what we’re about to talk about to them, or because of Consolidation later), they get to make an **Overrun Fight**. When doing this, the unit makes an additional Pile-In move prior to making attacks, giving them extra movement to tag their foes.

This can allow for some *incredibly* powerful Fight Phases for melee armies, because if they charge two alpha threats into a single target, and the first one swings and wipes them, the second unit gets to make an *additional* pile-in to reach new enemies and inflict carnage. Abilities that provide 6” Pile-Ins amplify this even more, and Berzerker Warband is going to do some absolutely *unholy* (or from Khorne’s point of view, *extremely* holy) things to unprepared players till they adjust to playing around this.

## Selecting Units to Fight

In the Fight Step, players take turns selecting units to Fight, starting with the Active player, and at any time if a player has no eligible units, they have to pass. First, all units with Fights First are selected (which includes those that Charged). If only one player has units with Fights First, they get to select all of these one after the other - the opponent can only pass.

After all Fight First units have been selected, other units get their chance. Importantly, the alternation continues straight onwards from Fight First units, so if one player selects the final Fight First unit, their opponent gets to pick the first “normal” fighter. If, at any point, a unit that has Fight First becomes eligible and hasn’t fought yet, then the step reverts to resolving Fight First units until all of those have run out again.

![](rules-deep-dive-the-charge-and-fight-phases-04.jpg)

Jain Zar & Howling Banshees. Credit: James "Boon" Kelling

The most important change here is that the Active player chooses the first Fight First unit, rather than it being the Opposing player. Charging a powerful Fight First unit is no longer a death sentence, and the Fight First ability is correspondingly a lot less good. Fight First has been *hugely* warping in high-level play all through 10th Edition, and this makes it much easier to handle, particularly if a player only has one unit with access to it.

It does also mean there’s a route for the Active Player to pick the first normal Fight if they desperately need to. If you don’t charge with any units and thus don’t have anyone with Fight First, then your first selection will be a normal unit, which you can use to bail them out of a tight spot (at a cost of starting other Fights).

## Making Attacks

When units are selected to Fight (either a regular or Overrun Fight), their models get to make attacks with their melee weapons against an enemy unit they’re engaged with. This works the pretty same as making shooting attacks, so check out our Shooting Phase article for more details. The only major difference is that rather than splitting weapons between targets, each model can split its attacks as it chooses between the targets that it is engaged with, and that you are obliged to use all attacks you have available to you.

## Consolidation

Once both players pass, because no eligible units remain, there is a consolidation step, where both players get to move their units again. Starting with all of the Active Player’s units, each unit that was eligible to fight this phase gets to consolidate, one at a time.

Consolidation is a modal ability, and each unit that does so must use the relevant mode below.

If a unit is currently engaged, they use **Ongoing Consolidation**. They select every enemy unit they are engaged with as targets, then each model that is not currently in base-to-base contact can move up to 3”. Each must end their move closer to the nearest selected target, and engaged with them as possible (similar to Pile-In).

If a unit is not engaged, but *is* within 3” of an enemy unit (***Note:*** *we are wondering if this is supposed to be 5”, and are hoping an FAQ will clarify)*, then they select **Engaging Consolidation**, and must select one or more of those enemy units within 3” as targets. They then move models up to 3”, which must follow the same conditions as above (and in addition, the unit must engage all the selected targets). If any of the targets reached have not yet been selected to Fight this phase, they immediately become eligible and then Fight (one at a time, if there’s more than one). This means that more Fights, including Overrun Fights, can be triggered during this step.

There’s an interesting nuance here that if the Active Player pulls new Opposing Player units into battle at this point, those *will* later get to Consolidate themselves (because the Opposing Player hasn’t had their part of the Consolidation step yet), but if the Opposing Player does so in their part of the step, it’s too late for the Active Player’s units to Consolidate (there’s no circuit breaker to go back to an earlier part of the step as for Fight First).

![](rules-deep-dive-the-charge-and-fight-phases-05.jpg)

Manufactorum Ruins. Credit: SRM

The final Consolidation mode can be selected if neither of the above applies, but the unit is within 3” of an Objective. In this case, they can use **Objective Consolidation**, and move each model up to 3”. Each model that moves must end the move either within range of the Objective (so, within it if it’s a terrain feature), or closer to it if that’s not possible. This does mean that if you’re using Terrain Objectives (the default for 11th Edition) and are already on the terrain, you can freely move 3” on it as long as you don’t leave the terrain. With 6” Consolidation effects, this can be quite powerful for scooting behind a wall, or adding some vital distance to try and get **Hidden** online.
