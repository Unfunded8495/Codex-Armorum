## Unit Positioning

The fundamentals of how units are positioned on the battlefield get two big changes in 11th Edition, which are going to be some of the biggest adjustments for returning players.

## Coherency

![Necron Warriors](rules-deep-dive-moving-and-the-movement-phase-01.jpg)

Necron Warriors. Credit: Pendulin

Any time a unit ends a move or is set up, it must be in Coherency, which features one of the biggest changes in 11th. Units are now in Coherency if every model is within 2” of at least one other model in the unit, and **every** model is within 9” of every other model in the unit. Essentially, draw a 9” diameter circle on the table, and every model’s base must be touching some part of it.

This means that stringing out a large unit in a giant conga line is fully dead, and even 5-model units can’t occupy quite as much space as they used to. This is a fairly heavy blow to horde lists, as it reduces the amount of trouble they can cause with one large squad, both in terms of move blocking and starting huge fights. It also makes fully screening the table *much* more difficult, especially in combination with a lower general Ingress range of 8” instead of 9”. For some mid-sized melee units, there can be some upside to this - it’s a bit easier to deploy with models pushed out in multiple directions to keep charge options open, and the new Charge and Engagement rules are generally pretty good for letting 10-ish model units maximise their Fighting.

## Engagement

Speaking of Engagement Range, this is now globally changed to 2” horizontal distance (still 5” vertically) *but* there’s no longer any restriction on moving through enemy Engagement Range with “standard” move types, as long as you end the move outside them. This takes some getting used to, as players have gotten very good at eyeballing 1” distances, and tactically it has the impact that it’s easier for a relatively small enemy unit to completely prevent you from moving onto an objective from a given direction, even though some of the objective footprints are bigger now. That can make melee threats a real tough balancing act for shooting armies to handle - if you want to flip their objective, you’ve got to leave enough alive that you can Charge, but not enough that they’ll then murder you. Alternatively, you can use an ability that lets you move after shooting, and those probably go up in value for just this reason!

Larger engagement can also have an impact on setting up out of Transports, but the rules here have been changed quite a bit to make it harder (though probably not impossible, read on later) to trap foes inside. On that note, all Set Up rules require models to be placed outside Engagement Range of enemies unless otherwise specified. Finally, **Engaged** is now a rules term - a model is **Engaged** if it is within range of one or more enemy models, and any unit that has any models within it Engaged is also said to be Engaged. Conversely, any unit to which this does not apply is Unengaged.

## Moving Models

11th updates the general rules for how models move, bringing some updates and changes that were added gradually to 10th into the Core Rules, and also streamlining a few other elements.

## Types of Move

![Assault Intercessor Sergeant](rules-deep-dive-moving-and-the-movement-phase-02.jpg)

Assault Intercessor Sergeant. Credit: Pendulin

A lot of rules in 11th Edition are presented as types of move, in a fancy new rules template. This includes the expected Movement Phase options like Normal Moves or Advances, but also covers Charges, Fight Phase Movement, and things that used to have their own category of rules like Disembarking from Transports or arriving from Reserves as an Ingress Move.

Each type of move will *either* include a Maximum Distance, for types of move that where models travel around the Battlefield, or a Set-Up Distance, which applies to types that Set Up a unit. Maximum Distance governs how far a unit can travel, while Set-Up Distance will be measured relative to some position, and determine where models must be placed.

Types of Move will also often have the following sets of conditions and/or triggers on them:

- **Before Moving:** Decisions you need to make before starting the move.
- **While Moving:** Restrictions you must follow while moving/placing individual models.
- **After Moving:** Conditions the whole unit must fulfil after all models have been moved, plus anything that triggers at this point.
In addition to any After Moving conditions specific to the move, at that point the unit must also be in Coherency.

For all types of move that use a Maximum Distance, you can always choose to move one or more of the models in your units. This can be relevant for moves like Charges that apply quite strict While and After moving conditions - if you don’t want a model to be pulled into an awkward position, you can leave it in place, as long as you can meet While Moving limitations for the models you do choose to move, and After Moving conditions for the unit.

Importantly, however, you sometimes won’t be able to do this - While Moving conditions are applied *first* while models are moving, and Coherency is only checked at the After Moving step, and if not met, the entire unit’s move is undone.

This means that if a move, for example, requires a model that moves to end in engaged with an an enemy unit, and doing so would pull them out of coherency with a model you don’t want to move, then your choice is to either move both models so that you meet the While Moving conditions for the first one, or leave the whole unit stationary - you can’t use the “threat” of coherency to sandbag on your obligations during the While Moving step.

## Straight Lines and Pivots

All movement is now executed as a series of straight lines and pivots, up to the total movement that the type of move you’re making allows. If, for example, a large model needs to move around a corner, you need to measure a straight line from its current position long enough to clear the back of its base/hull past the corner, then a second straight line to rocket it off in a new direction. This largely formalises how people already measured moves like this, but makes it very clear and explicit.

The bigger change is in pivots. When you pivot a model, you rotate it around its centre axis, and this **does not cost you any movement**. This can potentially add a good 1-2” of reach on especially large models if they start with their wider axis facing the foe, though that can make them trickier to hide. We assume that the reason that pivot costs are gone is that it no longer causes issues with changing Charge distances due to the new ways those are resolved, but you do need to be aware of potential extra reach from normal movement.

There are also no more diagonal FLYing moves, because…

## FLY

![](rules-deep-dive-moving-and-the-movement-phase-03.jpg)

Credit: Robert "TheChirurgeon" Jones

FLY gets a rework in 11th Edition, bringing it much closer to how it worked in older Editions. The FLY keyword now does nothing at baseline, not even letting you move through enemy models, but when making a Normal/Advance/Fall Back/Charge Move they can **Take to the Skies**. When they do, they subtract 2” from their maximum move distance, but can then freely move through all enemy models and all terrain for the duration of that move. In addition, they can ignore vertical distance when moving, so if they want to hop up to a higher floor in a ruin, there’s no additional cost beyond the 2”.

This is generally a massive boost for Monsters/Vehicles/Mounted units with FLY, and a minor downgrade (in most situations) for Infantry/Beast units. The latter could already move through walls, and now need to pay 2” to hop enemy models. The former can now push out from terrain *far* more quickly, putting some classic elf nonsense back on the table (and juicing up many big centrepiece monsters). The reduction in big, solid ruins in the official terrain set makes it slightly less of a step change than it could have been, but it’s still very good, and if any event packs stick to larger walls like current UKTC or WTC ones it’s going to be monstrously strong.

## Moving Through Gaps

Models that have a base can move through any space their base fits through, so you can ignore overhanging tentacles/reaper chainswords or whatever. This was added as rules errata in 10th, and is now a locked-in part of the core rules.

## Monsters and Vehicles

Monsters and Vehicles can now move through non-Monster/Vehicle enemy models when making Normal or Advance moves, as long as they end the move legally (so usually outside Engagement Range). Clearing Engagement Range is not trivial now that it’s 2” on either side, so this is mostly for faster models in the Movement Phase, but with a good Advance you can sometimes hop to a surprising position.

Monsters and Vehicles are also now affected by the FRAME keyword, which is the rules signifier for “measure to hull”. If such a model has no base, when pivoting it you rotate it around its central axis. There are a couple of weird edge cases here with some units that have bases and also FRAME which we expect to get cleared up in FAQ and Commentary.

## Movement Phase

11th Edition makes a few key changes to the flow of how the Movement Phase is executed.

## Start and End of Phase Steps

Like every Phase, the Movement Phase now has explicit Start and End of Phase steps, where abilities which trigger at these points are resolved. These are also governed by the new sequencing rules, which mean that all Active Player abilities are resolved before all Opposing Player ones.

## No Reinforcements Step

Structurally, the biggest change to the Movement Phase is that there’s no separate Reinforcements step. At the start of the Movement Phase, all units in your army, *including* those in Reserves or Transports, are eligible to make a Move, and where they are determines the types that they are eligible to make. Models in Reserves are only eligible to either Remain Stationary or make an **Ingress Move**, the latter of which is how you set up from reserves. You can resolve these at any point in the Movement Phase, which can have a small number of fringe applications if you have some sort of unit with a Movement buff aura waiting in the wings.

More on Ingress Moves and Transports further down.

***Note:*** *Some rules in 10th Edition Codexes specify that they take place in the Reinforcements step of the Movement Phase, so you may be wondering where these now happen. The extended version of the rules that’s going into the App includes a catch-all for these that they now happen in the Move Units step of this phase.*

## Overwatch Is at the End of the Phase

![](rules-deep-dive-moving-and-the-movement-phase-04.jpg)

Necron Hexmark Destroyer

We’ll cover this in more detail in the Core Stratagems and Abilities article, but Overwatch is no longer a reaction to movement - it’s now triggered in the End of the Movement Phase step. In terms of Movement Phase flow, this speeds things up considerably, because you no longer have to ask whether a foe is Overwatching each time you move, nor try to brain genius the sequencing to stop them doing so.

## Types of Move in the Movement Phase

11th Edition now has six types of move that units can make in your Movement Phase. These are:

- Remain Stationary
- Normal Move
- Advance Move
- Fall Back Move
- Ingress Move
- Disembark move
Four of these return from 10th Edition, and will be covered here, while Ingress Moves and Disembark Moves get their own sections below.

Each move type is laid out with the new 11th Edition templating, making it very clear what the eligibility, process and limitation for each is. One thing we’ll shout out here - perhaps the only thing we don’t like about these templates is that the distance limit is in a grey bar at the top, which the eye can skim over. Check that if you’re baffled where (for example) the bonus for Advancing lives!

## Remain Stationary

You do nothing. Pretty simple. The rules do make it clear that “nothing” includes no pivoting (that’s free during a move, but it’s still moving), and Remaining Stationary does not trigger any “ends a move” effects. In addition, any unit in Reserves or Transports that does not want to Ingress/Disembark is now Remaining Stationary.

## Normal Move

An unengaged unit can move up to their M” Characteristic, and can act normally for the rest of the turn. Simple, classic, unchanged from 10th.

## Advance Move

An unengaged unit can move up to their M” Characteristic +d6”, and afterwards are not eligible to perform an Action or Charge for the rest of the turn (and when Shooting, will only be able to select Assault Shooting by default). Once again, functionally unchanged from 11th, just clearer wording.

## Fall-Back Move

Fall-Back moves have the biggest change in 11th Edition, as selecting between a “normal” Fall Back (now called an **Ordered Retreat**) and a **Desperate Escape**. Fall Back is the only move type other than **Remain Stationary** you can select while Engaged, and is how you leave combat.

An **Ordered Retreat** can be selected if a unit is not currently Battle-shocked, and allows them to move up their M” Characteristic, following all normal rules for movement. After completing this move, the unit is not eligible to Shoot, perform an Action, or Charge for the rest of the turn.

If you either cannot select **Ordered Retreat** because your unit is Battle-shocked, or need to move through an enemy model in order to escape combat, you can select a **Desperate Escape**. This allows a unit to move up to their M” Characteristic, and move through enemy models while doing so, but every model in the unit must make a Hazard roll, and if the unit is not *already* Battle-shocked, they have to take a Battle-shock test afterwards.

This changes the calculus on Desperate Escape quite a bit from 10th. On the one hand, it’s now less directly lethal to Monsters and Vehicles, who will never just *die* unless they were already on the brink, and multi-wound Infantry also suffer less casualties per dice rolled. On the flip side, the whole unit now has to take Hazard rolls even if only one model needs to Escape, and the Battle-shock test has the potential to throw plans into disarray. In the aggregate, wrapping enemy models is scarier again now, so dust-off that tri-pointing technique!

## Ingress Moves and Reserves

![](rules-deep-dive-moving-and-the-movement-phase-05.jpg)

Imperial Fists Terminator Assault Squad. Credit: Jack Hunter

11th Edition introduces the term **Ingress Move** to cover the process of a unit arriving on the Battlefield from Reserves, or being repositioned by teleportation or similar.

## What Is an Ingress Move?

An Ingress Move formalises the process of arriving on the battlefield during it. Models in Strategic Reserves can make a default Ingress Move, which allows them to be set up on the Battlefield:

- In Coherency.
- >8” Horizontally from all enemy units.
- Wholly within 6” of a battlefield edge.
- Not within your opponent’s Deployment Zone if it is before the third Battle Round.
The Deep Strike ability now works by modifying the restrictions on an Ingress Move, allowing you to be set up **anywhere** on the Battlefield that is >8” from enemy units, without the Battlefield Edge or Deployment Zone limitations.

Newly authored abilities that reposition a unit also use Ingress Moves. An example of this is the new Weirdboy Datasheet in the Armageddon box, where **Da Jump** works by removing a unit from the Battlefield, placing them in Reserves, then immediately allowing them to make an Ingress move.

## No Shooting Phase Movement After Ingress

![](rules-deep-dive-moving-and-the-movement-phase-06.jpg)

Scourges with shardcarbines. Credit: Corrode

A big change in 11th Edition is a return to the status quo of pre-10th Editions: if you make an Ingress Move, then until the next Charge Phase you are not eligible to make any type of Move. This means that you **cannot** arrive, blast something, then use an ability allowing movement after shooting to scoot back behind a wall (or sweep forward to claim an objective). We were, if we’re honest, never super clear why anything thought it was a good idea for this limitation to go away in 10th, but this will be a somewhat painful (re)adjustment for some mobile factions.

## Strategic Reserves Limits

Just like in the previous two Editions, you can choose for part of your army to begin in Reserves even if they don’t have Deep Strike or any other ability. What’s new in 11th is that there’s now just a single 1000pts limit on this (or half your army, for other game sizes), no separation between Deep Strike and Strategic Reserves.

Limitations on when Strategic Reserves can arrive are now part of the Core Rules as well - no Ingress Moves before the second Battle Round unless otherwise stated, and any unit that starts the game in Strategic Reserves that has not been set up by the end of Battle Round 3 is Destroyed (excluding units that are embarked in a Transport that *has* arrived from Reserves, they just haven’t Disembarked yet).

## Repositioned Units

Units placed into Reserves during the battle now have a blanket name - Repositioned Units. Somewhere my Editor is hoping, praying that this means he will never have to read “uppy-downy” used as a term of art ever again. Repositioned units are exempt from the requirement that units in Reserves must arrive by Battle Round 3, and have some sensible safety net clarifications about how ongoing effects work with them.

## Transports

![](rules-deep-dive-moving-and-the-movement-phase-07.jpg)

Credit: Robert "TheChirurgeon" Jones

Many armies have cool trucks (or cultural equivalents) that their forces can ride around in. 11th Edition makes some important changes to how these work. Transports still have a Transport Capacity that determines how many models and of what kind can ride in them at any given time.

## Embarking

By default, a unit that finishes a Normal, Advance or Fall Back Move with every model within 3” of a Transport can Embark within it as long as there is sufficient spare Transport Capacity to accommodate all the unit’s models.

However, there is a **very** important new limitation on this - you cannot do this (by default) if you were Set Up on the Battlefield this turn. Since Disembarking from a Transport involves being set up, this means that Fire and Fade type abilities now *globally* do not work to let you get out, shoot, then jump back in. Grey Knight Interceptors in absolute shambles. No more gradually adding this limitation to more and more Stratagems/Abilities via FAQ - it’s just a Core Rule.

## Disembarking

![](rules-deep-dive-moving-and-the-movement-phase-08.jpg)

Ultramarines Intercessor and Repulsor. Credit: SRM

Disembark is now a type of move, and uses the modal ability template to provide three different options. Two of these systematise what you could do in 10th Edition with some tweaks, one is entirely new.

Because these are now types of Move, they “use up” your unit’s Move for the turn, so unless otherwise specified, you cannot make another move after using them.

Your first option is a **Rapid Disembark**, which you are eligible to do on a unit Embarked in a Transport that has either made a Normal Move or Ingress Move this phase. You set up the unit wholly within 3” of the Transport, and they are not eligible to Charge this turn. If you did this after an Ingress, you must follow any set up restrictions that the Transport had to follow while making that move. This *mostly* just systematises 10th Edition Rules for disembarking after Moving/arriving from Reserves, but on the latter it tightens up the conditions by applying *all* the same limitations to the Disembarking unit. Previously, the distance from the enemy applied, but not other conditions, so you could (e.g.) disembark a unit onto an objective that was slightly more than 6” from the Battlefield Edge when arriving from Strategic Reserves.

Second is the **Tactical Disembark**, which you perform either before the Transport has been selected to move, or on a Transport that has Remained Stationary. Here, you set your unit up wholly within 3”, as above, then that unit can *immediately* also make a Normal Move or Advance Move. This is 11th’s version of Disembarking before moving, and is *mostly* the same, but requiring the unit to move immediately after the Disembark changes things a little, because they have to do their move with the vehicle still in its starting position. This means you can’t ever end up with a unit that Disembarked in a position that the vehicle was originally occupying, so you sometimes need to plan ahead with how you’re staging behind terrain to account for this.

In both of the above cases, if a model’s base is >3”, then when disembarking the condition for them is changed to just require that they be within 1” of the Transport. This can give a smidge of extra reach to models like Ghazghkull.

Our final option here is new - the **Combat Disembark**. You can only select this if neither of the above apply - you haven’t moved *and* it’s not possible to set up your unit within 3” of your Transport, usually because of enemy Engagement Range. In this case, you can instead set up your unit wholly within 6” of your Transport, and can be in Engagement Range of one or more enemy units that are currently Engaged with the Transport. This comes at a cost, of course - your whole unit has to make Hazard Rolls *and* the unit takes a Battle-shock test, so you probably won’t be giving your foe your best shot!

## Emergency Disembark

Emergency Disembarks happen when a Transport is destroyed, and allow the models within it to be Set Up. When doing so, they must be wholly within 6” of the Transport, Unengaged, and also as close to it as possible. This is a change from 10th, clearly designed to give units a bit of breathing room to avoid larger Engagement Ranges without giving them free reign to rocket boost into the distance. It does, notably, make it *much* harder to avoid being tagged in the situation where two enemies charge a Transport, and the first wrecks it. Although the spacing is quite generous, 2” engagement range on either side of enemies is a *huge* amount of zoning, and something we’ll be getting our diagram wizards to look at is whether there are some configurations where a couple of units (particularly on oval bike bases) operating in concert can sufficiently surround a normal-sized transport to make this disembark impossible, and obliterate the models within.

Even if that doesn’t happen, going through this process is traumatic, and the passengers must make Hazard Rolls, and are Battle-shocked (which is more of a downside now that it can persist through the next Command Phase).

## Wrap Up

Lots of changes here, and plenty for players to absorb, but a *lot* of what’s here is ultimately designed to simplify and streamline things, and we think once players adjust to 11th, the Movement Phase will play out quite a bit faster. The functional changes to Coherency and Engagement will also have *big* impacts on the game as a whole, as will some of the new limitations that prevent units from acting with such impunity.
