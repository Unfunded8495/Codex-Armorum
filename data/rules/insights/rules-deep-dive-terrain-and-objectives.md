## Terrain

Terrain was one of the bits of 10th Edition that never really *quite* landed. 11th gives it a full overhaul, and also centres it even more in battles because key terrain areas on the table now take on the role of Objectives. Terrain can also protect models by providing Cover, blocking Visibility for shooting, or by letting light forces hunker down with the new **Hidden** rule.

Terrain is a particularly important topic, with tonnes of detail to go into. As well as this initial deep dive, the Ruleshammer team are hard at work preparing a full guide to terrain in 11th Edition, featuring loads of helpful diagrams to help you get to grips with the new rules.

## Terrain Features and Terrain Areas

When placing terrain on the battlefield, there are two components:

- **Terrain Feature:** The actual, physical piece of terrain.
- **Terrain Area:** a mat, template (such as from the new terrain areas pack GW have released) or agreed area that terrain features sit within.

![](rules-deep-dive-terrain-and-objectives-01.jpg)

A terrain feature (the ruin) on a Games Workshop cardboard terrain area. Credit: Wings

The boundaries of the Terrain Area determine whether models are within the terrain for rules purposes, and also have a rules-based impact on Visibility drawn across them. The physical Terrain Features can impact on how models move across terrain areas, and also affect “true” Visibility to models in or around the Terrain Area.

## Terrain Objectives

Warhammer 40K battles are often fought over Objectives, vital areas of the battlefield that need to be secured. In 11th Edition, rather than these being independent areas or locations, the default is that one or more Terrain Areas on the battlefield is designated as an Objective. We’ll talk about controlling them later on in the article, but the important thing to know up front when reading about the Terrain rules is that holding Objectives now generally means placing yourself within a Terrain Area.

## Terrain Categories

Terrain Features are split into three categories:

- **Exposed:** ground-level features like craters that don’t affect movement or visibility at all.
- **Light:** Small walls, piles of crates, minor decorative statues.
- **Dense:** Large ruins, buildings, big Munitorum Containers.
All Models can move through Light and Exposed terrain freely, whatever the size of the physical Feature. For Dense Terrain Features, INFANTRY, SWARMS and BEASTS can move through it freely, but other models must move around it, excluding parts of it that are <2” tall, which can also be moved through freely.

All Dense terrain also has the **Solid** rule. This has impacts on Visibility, and for movement means that models cannot end their move with any part of the model poking out through an enclosed gap in the terrain (e.g. a window) that’s <3” from the ground.

![](rules-deep-dive-terrain-and-objectives-02.jpg)

Terrain areas with different types of features. Credit: Wings

A Terrain Area can have multiple Terrain Features of different categories placed on it, and indeed this is a feature of the terrain layouts that Games Workshop have included to go with Chapter Approved. Sometimes this means lighter forces can dash through the centre of an area, while tanks and monstrosities have to burn some movement to wiggle around. You can see this in the image above - on the left, we have an area containing a single Dense feature, and on the right, one with a single Light feature. The middle area contains three features: two Light (the pipes) and one Dense (the large statue). All three of these setups are part of the Chapter Approved layouts.

**Light** and **Dense** Terrain Features also bequeath rules on Terrain Areas that they are within. Any area containing a Feature with either of these categories becomes **Obscuring** (impacting visibility through it) and makes the Area eligible for light forces to be **Hidden** within it. We’ll go through what all that means in this article.

## Cover

One of the key things that terrain provides is **Cover,** which inflicts a -1BS on ranged attacks targeting units.

A unit has cover against an attacking model if one of the following applies to every model within it:

- The model has the INFANTRY/SWARM/BEAST keyword and is within a Terrain Area
- The model is not fully visible to the attacker because of either an intervening Terrain Feature, or a partially intervening Terrain Area thanks to the Obscuring rule.
A really important change from 10th Edition is that Cover is now determined per attacking model, rather than all or nothing for the whole unit. If half of an attacking unit has full Visibility on a target, and half does not, then you split those two groups up and resolve their attacks separately, half with -1BS and half without. It also means that you have to think a bit more carefully about positioning your units defensively, because even one model fully exposed in the open takes Cover away from your entire unit, whereas in 10th you could do that, and still take saves on models with the Cover benefit (which in 10th was a reduction in AP).

This tends to mean that units will have Cover less often than they did in 10th Edition, and changing the penalty to affect the hit roll rather than AP means that attacks which *do* get through are more dangerous. AP-1 weaponry gets *vastly* better in 11th Edition, and mid-AP stuff with **Sustained Hits** (which mitigates the impact of the penalty) is especially strong.

## Hidden

![Skitarii Vanguard Taking Cover](rules-deep-dive-terrain-and-objectives-03.jpg)

Skitarii Vanguard Taking Cover. Credit: Pendulin

Hidden is one of the marquee new mechanics for 11th Edition, allowing INFANTRY, SWARM and BEAST units to sacrifice their shooting to conceal themselves in terrain areas.

***Important Note:*** *There are some changes to Hidden between what’s in the rule book and the updated version of the rules that’s going to be pushed to the app, specifically that areas with only Light features* ***are*** *eligible for Hidden and the addition of the* ***Gone to Ground*** *rule. If you are reading this and thinking it’s slightly different to what you’ve seen elsewhere, that might be why!*

A model with one of the qualifying keywords is Hidden if it is within a Terrain Area that contains at least one Light or Dense terrain feature, and that model’s unit has not made any Ranged Attacks this turn or the previous turn (if there was one, so you can be Hidden at the start of the battle). While Hidden, a model is not Visible to enemy units that are outside of its **Detection Range**, which is normally 15”.

Detection Range can be modified by rules on Datasheets (plenty of the previewed Detachments on Warhammer Community interact with it), and can also be impacted by the core **Gone to Ground** rule. If a model is Hidden, their unit did not make any ranged attacks, and is not fully visible to the attacking model because of an intervening Dense/Solid terrain feature, then Detection range is reduced by 3” to 12”. Notably, the requirement to not make Ranged Attacks here is explicitly separate to that for Hidden. Some units have rules that allow them to make Ranged Attacks and stay Hidden, but in this case they will **not** get the additional Gone to Ground benefit.

Hidden is something completely new, and is going to be crucial to the dynamics of 11th Edition, as it’s now your premier way to try and hold objectives safely. You are going to see a lot of Infantry nervously stepping a few models onto the side of a Terrain Area furthest from the opponent, ideally partially behind a Solid wall to drop the Detection Range to 12”. This can be pretty effective and safe - if the other models stay behind the terrain, they’ll not be Visible because of the Obscuring rule, and 12” is a pretty short distance for foes to have to reach, at least in the early turns of a game. From our experiments with the new edition, we’ve found that it’s especially good for Infantry units with **Scout**, as it generally broadens their options for where they can safely move out of the gate. As battle progresses, Hidden generally becomes harder to use, though can still have some crucial impacts.

Exactly how much this matters is something we’re going to discover as people play a *lot* more games, but we’ll say that the **Gone to Ground** rule feels like it makes a big difference compared to our initial impressions. 3” may not sound like a big change, but on the Chapter Approved layouts there’s plenty of positions where 15” *will* let you shoot from one Terrain Objective to pretty much the whole of another with impunity, and 12” *won’t*. It’s also *exceptionally* good for units like Space Marine Infiltrators that prevent foes from arriving from Reinforcements within 12”, as that can make them really hard to get a full shooting swing on. Units that can improve it further also love this, as you can get it as low as 9” before hitting the modifier cap, at which point you’re a real pain to pry out. We do still worry a bit that against optimised armies full of fast shooting hulls it doesn’t do enough to offset the changes to Obscuring that 11th brings, but it’s certainly felt like it’s adding some new dynamics in our test games.

Also, if you’re planning on using Overwatch with a unit, don’t forget that it will turn their ability to be Hidden off!

## Obscuring

![](rules-deep-dive-terrain-and-objectives-04.jpg)

Munitorum Armored Containers. Credit: SRM

Terrain Areas that contain Light or Dense features are Obscuring. This means that if neither the firing model or the target is within the Terrain Area, then it blocks visibility, thus allowing units to fully protect themselves behind an Area as long as their foe can’t step foot in it.

We’ve had rules like this in the last two Editions, but 11th does make a big change from 10th by requiring a firing model to only be **within** a terrain area to ignore it, rather than wholly within. The size and shapes of the official Terrain pack essentially means this *has* to be how things work, but *wow* does this open tables right up to large shooting pieces, especially if they can give themselves Assault somehow. This is probably our biggest structural concern with 11th Edition, full stop, particularly because a lot of the maps (24/45, for those counting) have a single giant 10”x10” ruin in the middle of the board, so if you can touch that it transforms your sightlines onto the table. The way other features are placed on the maps does *try* to mitigate this, and there are some very obvious “stage your forces here” positions, but I’m definitely not looking forward to experiencing my first Mont’ka full send in 11th. I expect to see an increased use of sacrificial chaff leveraging their 2” engagement range just to stop foes getting part of their model into Areas that provide key sightlines.

## Climbing Terrain

Many Terrain Features have upper floors or raised portions that models can be placed on. As long as a unit has the INFANTRY/SWARM/BEAST/FLY or MONSTER keywords, they can end a move on an upper floor as long as they fit stably, and their base isn’t overhanging the edge of whatever they’re sitting on. Monsters being able to do this is new for 11th Edition, and while most of them are going to be too big to fit on a lot of Features, if you’re playing on a table with a Skyshield Landing Pad and want your Carnifex to go out like King Kong, you can now live that dream.

11th also codifies *how* models should climb to upper floors. If you are within 0.5” horizontally of a Terrain Feature, you can move vertically straight up and down to climb it. Simple.

## Plunging Fire

Going to upper floors can expose your models, but has advantages. If a model is on a section of a terrain feature that is >3” tall, and is firing at a visible target with one or more models at ground level, their shooting attacks get +1BS, neatly cancelling out the benefits of cover (or providing a boost in lethality against targets in the open).

Getting models to 3” above ground level is a *much* easier ask than the 6” required in 10th Edition, so this will actually come up in more than 1% of games!

## Objectives

## Controlling Objectives

As discussed above, the default option for Objectives in 11th Edition is that you designate Terrain Areas on the Battlefield as them. A model is within range of a Terrain Objective if they are (unsurprisingly) within the Terrain Area, and at the end of each phase and the end of the turn, control of an Objective is determined by which player has the highest Level of Control over it. Level of Control is calculated by adding up the OC of all models that are within range of the objective, obviously accounting for any modifiers or things that remove OC like Battle-shock.

Some rules also check whether a specific unit is controlling an objective. This is the case if a unit has a model with at least OC1 within range of an objective that the unit’s owner controls (so interestingly, an OC0 unit can never be affected by such rules, even if it’s alongside a unit with OC1 that *is* controlling an objective).

## Objective Secured

![Imperial Fist Intercessors](rules-deep-dive-terrain-and-objectives-05.jpg)

Imperial Fist Intercessors. Credit: Jack Hunter

Some rules allow an Objective to be **Secured**, which means it remains under one player’s control until their opponent has a higher Level of Control on it at the end of any phase. These rules existed in 10th Edition, but were written out separately each time, so this is a nice bit of streamlining. It’s also a *very* important rule in 11th Edition because of how much more dangerous holding objectives (sometimes even your home objective) can be, even with Hidden. If you can secure an objective, then if your opponent can’t reach out and grab it it stays yours, even if they blast you to smithereens.

## Types of Objective

11th Edition’s Chapter Approved missions introduce three different designations of Objectives, which incorporate some unofficial 10th Edition lingo into the actual rules. Objectives in Chapter Approved are either:

- **Home Objectives:** Applied to a single objective in each player’s deployment zone.
- **Expansion Objectives:** Applied to two objectives that are in no-man’s land, but closer to one player’s deployment zone, making it safer for them to move out and capture it.
- **Central Objectives:** Applied to either a single central objective (on 30/45 maps) or two separate objectives slightly offset from the centre (the other 15).
Each official layout clearly labels which objectives are which.

Many Primary and Secondary Missions specify which type of objective you need to control for different conditions, with the greatest rewards often attached to the Central Objective(s) because of the higher risk of controlling them.

## Non-Terrain Objectives

In the appendix of the rulebook, there’s a rule for how to play with Objectives that are not Terrain Areas, which is to use a 40mm marker with a 3” control radius, exactly the same as in 10th Edition. This is clearly just there as a fallback - it’s not the intended way to play 11th Edition.
