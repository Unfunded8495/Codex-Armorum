How to Actually Play

From a blank table to the final score. A walkthrough and table companion for 11th Edition, illustrated at every step.

Jump to the battle round Just tell me what to do first

The Core Rules explain every rule in isolation, but they never tell you what to physically do first, second and third on game day. This guide does. It is written for players who know the shape of the game but do not play every week: alongside the walkthrough, each chapter carries the timings and traps that occasional players forget between games. Section numbers in brackets (like 07.02) match the reference numbers used in the Core Rules, and the Rules Insights page holds the full community articles this guide draws on.

01What You Needbefore game day

A game of Warhammer 40,000 is two armies fighting over objectives on a terrain-covered table, for a set number of turns. To play, gather:

  • Two armies of Citadel miniatures, one per player, built to an agreed points size (see the next chapter).
  • An army list for each player: which units, which wargear, which enhancements. Build and check yours in the Army Builder.
  • A table with terrain. The mission's deployment map defines the battlefield size and the two deployment zones.
  • Terrain: ruins, containers, woods, crates. More is better than less; a bare table heavily favours shooting armies.
  • Dice (a good handful of D6), a tape measure in inches, and something to mark objectives.
  • A mission, generated before you begin, from a mission deck or the Missions page.

Read a datasheet first

Everything a unit can do lives on its datasheet: its characteristics (Movement, Toughness, Save, Wounds, Objective Control and so on), its weapons, its abilities and its keywords. You will have one per unit in your army. Learn to read one and most of the game falls into place.

Every model you own has its full datasheet in the Weapon Loadouts and catalogue views on this site.

02Muster Your Armylist building

Missions tell you to muster an army to a total points size. The three standard battle sizes are:

Battle sizePointsDetachmentsEnhancementsDuplicate limit

Strike Force2,000343

Strike Force (2,000 points) is the default for most games. Mustering means: pick an army faction (your Warlord must share its faction keyword), pick your detachment (its rules, enhancements and stratagems), add units up to the points limit (the duplicate limit is doubled for Battleline and Dedicated Transports), then choose wargear, enhancements and which unit each Leader will join.

Character attachment is permanent (19). Leaders join their bodyguard unit at muster, not on game day, and the attached unit stays one unit for the whole battle, even if the leader or every bodyguard model dies. A unit can hold one Leader plus one Support character (Support characters must attach; they can never deploy alone). The attached unit shares every datasheet's keywords, but a rule needing a keyword stops working once every model with it is dead, and a leader's buffs switch off if the leader is sniped out with Precision attacks.

03Setting Up the Tablebefore the first turn

Your mission card lists the exact setup sequence; below is the shape almost every matched-play mission takes. Do all of this before any dice are rolled in anger.

1 · Generate the mission

Before anything is placed, settle on a primary mission (how you score most points), a deployment map (below), a mission layout (where terrain and objectives sit) and, if you both agree, a twist (a battle-wide rule such as Night Fighting). 2 · Build the battlefield

Place terrain following the layout, or by agreement (13.01). Each cluster of terrain (or the mat it sits on) defines a terrain area. Terrain comes in three categories (13.02): Exposed (craters, debris: no real cover), Light (barricades, low walls: can give cover, little obstruction) and Dense (buildings, ruins, woods: blocks sight and movement, and matters most). When in doubt, add more dense terrain and leave room around it for big models.

The official Event Companion publishes the exact terrain area footprints used at Games Workshop events: four 6″×4″, two 10″×2.5″, four 6″×2″, four 7″×11.5″ and two 8″×11.5″ polygonal areas, each feature designated light or dense. It deliberately leaves space between a feature and the edge of its area so a line of models can stand on the area “from the outside”: that gap is what makes toeing onto objectives possible. Copy the pattern and your table plays as the designers intended.

3 · Place objectives

The map shows several objective points. Each should sit on a terrain area; that whole area is the objective, a terrain objective (14.01). A model is within range of it while it is within that area. Standing on objectives is how you win, so mark them clearly. The official layouts label each objective Home (in a deployment zone), Expansion (no man's land, offset toward one player) or Central; missions usually pay the most for Central because it is the most dangerous to hold.

{{ fig('diagram-controlling-a-terrain-objective.jpg', 1204, 1145, 'Controlling a terrain objective', 'Each player totals the Objective Control of their models in the area; higher total controls it.') }}

4 · Roll off, deploy, begin

This is the official pre-battle order from the Event Companion, used verbatim at Games Workshop events and the shape every matched play game should follow:

  1. Roll off to decide Attacker and Defender (the mission says what each picks).
  2. Declare Battle Formations. Both players note down secretly, then reveal together: which units start embarked in which transports, and which are in strategic reserves (20.01), including Deep Strikers. Reserves are capped at 1,000 points at Strike Force (half your army at other sizes), one shared pool. Aircraft must start in reserves and count against the cap (23.01).
  3. Deploy your armies, alternating one unit at a time within your own zone, Defender first. Setting up a TITANIC unit costs you your next set-up turn; when one player finishes, the other places everything remaining.
  4. Redeploy units. Redeployment rules resolve now, alternating, Attacker first. A unit a redeploy puts into strategic reserves does not count against the reserves cap.
  5. Determine the first turn: roll off, winner takes it. The same player takes the first turn every battle round (07.02).
  6. Resolve Pre-battle Rules, alternating, first-turn player first (Scout moves happen here, 24.31; Scouts no longer work for units deployed outside your own zone). Then begin the battle.
Playing at an event?

Warhammer Events skip Deployment and Twist cards. When mustering you select a Force Disposition card available to your detachment; at the table, your opponent's disposition symbol on your own card names your primary mission, and each pairing of primaries has three fixed layouts (A, B, C). Those pairings and layouts are the mission presets on the Missions page.

04The Battle Roundthe heart of the game

The game runs in battle rounds (usually five). In each round the first player takes a full turn, then the second player takes a full turn (07). Every turn moves through five phases in this fixed order:

1Command 2Movement 3Shooting 4Charge 5Fight

Command phase (08)

Both players gain 1 Command Point (yes, both, every turn). Then the active player makes a battle-shock roll for each unit that is at or below half strength (at counts, so even two-model units test), or already battle-shocked (08.03). While a unit is battle-shocked its Objective Control becomes “-”, an unmodifiable zero that no +1 OC buff can rescue, so it stops holding objectives; it also cannot be targeted by its own stratagems and cannot start or finish actions. A failed unit stays shaken and keeps testing until a battle-shock roll succeeds. Insane Bravery auto-passes one test per battle, but only if used before the roll and never on an already-shocked unit (shocked units cannot be targeted by their own stratagems). Command abilities resolve after battle-shock, so model-returning effects will not dodge this phase's test, and the mission-scoring sub-step closes the phase after everything else.

{{ fig('diagram-battle-shock-examples.jpg', 711, 1766, 'Battle-shock examples', 'Which units test, and what failing means.') }}

Movement phase (09)

Move your units one at a time. Each picks a move type: Remain Stationary (truly stationary, not even a free pivot), a Normal Move (up to its Move characteristic), an Advance (Move + D6, then only Assault weapons can shoot and no charging or actions), or a Fall Back to escape combat. Falling back is modal (09.07): an Ordered Retreat (only if not battle-shocked) means no shooting, actions or charging this turn; a Desperate Escape (forced if battle-shocked or moving through enemy models) costs every model a hazard roll plus a battle-shock test.

Units end moves in coherency (2″ to a neighbour and the whole unit inside a 9″ circle, 03.03) and cannot end a move in engagement range (2″ horizontally, 5″ vertically), though standard moves may now pass through it freely. Pivots cost nothing. Monsters and vehicles can move through non-monster, non-vehicle enemy models on a normal or advance move, so chaff rings no longer trap them; FLY models can pay 2″ of movement to pass through everything and ignore vertical distance.

Reserves arrive here with an ingress move (20.04): more than 8″ from all enemies, wholly within 6″ of a battlefield edge, and outside the enemy zone before round three; Deep Strike drops the edge and zone limits. Nothing arrives in round one, anything still in strategic reserves after round three is destroyed, and an ingressed unit cannot move again until its next Charge phase: no arriving, shooting, then stepping back behind a wall. Transports (18): embark by ending a move with all models within 3″ (never in the same turn the unit was set up, so no hop out and back in); disembarking is itself the unit's move for the turn, before the transport moves (then move immediately) or after it moved (no charging).

Their window

The end of your Movement phase is your opponent's moment: Rapid Ingress and Fire Overwatch both trigger there. Finish your moves expecting a unit to drop in or shoot.

Shooting phase (10)

Pick each eligible unit in turn and choose its shooting type (10.04 to 10.07): Normal (did not advance, unengaged), Assault (advanced; Assault weapons only), Close-Quarters (engaged: monsters and vehicles can shoot anything at -1 to hit and lose the Heavy bonus; others fire only Pistol or Close-Quarters weapons at what they are engaged with) or Indirect (Indirect Fire weapons may target the unseen, but the target gets cover, no hit re-rolls, and only unmodified 6s hit unless you stayed stationary with a friendly spotter unit seeing the target).

Select the weapons, select target units that are visible, in range and unengaged, then resolve the attacks (below). The visible model and the in-range model no longer need to be the same one, and Rapid Fire or Melta half-range checks happen at the Select Targets step against the unit, so resolving other weapons first cannot switch them off. Targets are locked before any dice are rolled.

{{ fig('diagram-model-visible.jpg', 960, 424, 'Model visible', 'Any part seen means the model is visible.') }} {{ fig('diagram-model-fully-visible.jpg', 956, 425, 'Model fully visible', 'The whole model can be seen.') }} {{ fig('diagram-unit-visible.jpg', 958, 421, 'Unit visible', 'At least one model in the unit is visible.') }} {{ fig('diagram-unit-fully-visible.jpg', 957, 418, 'Unit fully visible', 'Every model in the unit is visible.') }} {{ fig('diagram-benefit-of-cover.jpg', 589, 1144, 'Benefit of cover', 'Cover worsens the shooter\'s BS by 1; it does not improve the save.') }} {{ fig('diagram-hidden-and-obscuring.jpg', 593, 1143, 'Hidden and obscuring', 'Dense terrain blocks sight; a hidden unit is only seen within 15 inches.') }}

Terrain changes who can be hit. In 11th Edition the benefit of cover does not improve a save; instead it worsens the shooter's Ballistic Skill by 1 (a harder hit roll), and it applies to Infantry, Beasts or Swarm units in a terrain area or units screened by intervening terrain (13.08). Two traps: every model in the target unit must qualify (one model in the open strips the whole squad), and it is judged per attacking model, so an attacking unit can split into penalised and clean groups. A hidden Infantry, Beasts or Swarm model in a light or dense terrain area whose unit has not shot this turn or last can only be seen from within its detection range: 15″, or 12″ if gone to ground behind solid terrain (13.09, 13.11). High ground bites back: models more than 3″ up get Plunging Fire, +1 BS against ground-level targets, cancelling their cover (Towering models get it automatically within 12″). Shooting at an engaged monster or vehicle is always legal, at -1 to hit. Finally, a unit that shoots cannot start an action this turn, and vice versa.

The attack sequence (05): how every attack resolves

Shooting and fighting both use the same four steps. Roll each step's dice together:

  1. Hit rolls: one D6 per attack. Meet or beat the weapon's BS (shooting) or WS (melee). An unmodified 1 always misses; an unmodified 6 is a Critical Hit.
  2. Wound rolls: one D6 per hit, comparing Strength to Toughness (see the table in Quick Reference). Unmodified 1 fails, unmodified 6 is a Critical Wound.
  3. Save rolls: the defender groups the target's models (each character alone, then one group per identical Wounds/Save/invulnerable profile), declares the allocation order (wounded non-character group first, characters never before non-characters), then rolls one D6 per wound and resolves them lowest first (05.03). Putting worse-saved models early soaks the low dice. Invulnerable saves are not optional: the roll checks against both saves and fails only if it beats neither. Precision weapons can force a visible character's group to the front of the queue.
  4. Inflict damage: each failed save costs a model the weapon's Damage. Feel No Pain rolls happen per wound lost (Damage 3 means three dice). At 0 wounds a model is destroyed. Excess damage does not carry to the next model.

Modifiers stack in a fixed order (02.02): set values first (a 0 or “-” stops everything), then multiply, add, divide, subtract, rounding up at the end. This is why Melta no longer beats half-damage effects, and why nothing rescues battle-shocked Objective Control.

Charge phase (11)

To fight in melee a unit must charge, and the order matters. First pick an eligible unit: within 12″ of an enemy, not already engaged, and it has not advanced or fallen back this turn. Then roll 2D6 for the maximum distance (11.02). Only after the roll do you choose targets: enemy units within 12″ and within the distance you rolled, measured point to point (11.04). A unit 10″ away is not a legal target on a roll of 9, even though 9″ of movement would reach its 2″ engagement bubble; once a target is legal you get about 2″ of slack. There is no base-to-base requirement (staying off base contact is often right, because based models cannot pile in next phase), and you may roll, dislike your options and decline to move. The charge succeeds only if the unit can end engaged with every target it chose, without touching anything it did not. Fall short and the unit does not move at all. Charging pays off: chargers strike with Fights First. Deep Strike charges still need a 9, since you set up more than 8″ away.

No overwatch here

Overwatch moved to the end of the Movement phase. The opponent's counterplay in this phase is Heroic Intervention at the end of it, so your “safe” unit on an objective may still get counter-charged.

{{ fig('diagram-making-a-charge-move.jpg', 1201, 974, 'Making a charge move', 'Roll 2D6 first, then choose targets within the distance rolled.') }}

Fight phase (12)

Both players act here, in three steps. Pile In (12.02): all pile-ins happen before any fighting; the active player moves every eligible unit, then the opponent does, often into whatever cage was just built. Up to 3″, ending closer to (and if possible engaged with) the closest target; models already in base contact cannot pile in at all. Fight (12.04): eligibility locks at the start of this step, so a unit whose foes were all just killed still fights, making an overrun fight with a bonus pile-in (12.06). Fights First units go first (charging grants it) and the active player picks first, alternating; then everyone else. Only models within engagement range swing, all attacks must be used, and every unit that can fight must. Consolidate (12.07): a final 3″ move whose mode is dictated by the situation: toward the nearest engaged enemy, into an unengaged enemy within 3″ (who then immediately fights, if they have not), or within an objective you are near.

Chain wipes

Two units charging the same target is how massacres happen: the first wipes it, the second overruns 3″ into fresh victims. Conversely, leaving exactly one enemy model alive within 2″ of one of yours denies the overrun: only that model swings back.

05Scoring & Winningvictory points

Kills alone do not win games; victory points do, and most come from holding objectives.

Controlling an objective (14.02): at the end of each phase and turn, each player adds up the Objective Control (OC) of all their models within the objective's terrain area. Highest total controls it; a tie means nobody holds it unless it is secured (14.03), in which case the previous holder keeps it, even with the garrison wiped out, until actually out-controlled. Battle-shocked models contribute nothing, and rules asking whether a unit controls an objective need a model with OC 1 or more from that unit in range.

Primary mission: your card says when and how you score, usually for holding objectives at the end of your turn or the battle round. The End of Turn and End of Battle Round steps exist so you remember to check (07.03).

Secondary missions score alongside the primary. Before the battle each player secretly picks Fixed (two Fixed-marked cards, face up all battle, never discarded) or Tactical (draw two face up at the start of each of your Command phases; once per battle spend 1 CP at the end of your Command phase to redraw one). At the end of each player's turn check your active cards: score what you achieved (Tactical cards are then discarded), and on your own turn you may discard one or more unwanted Tactical cards to gain 1 CP. “When Drawn” sections only apply to Tactical play.

Actions (16) are battlefield tasks some missions need. A unit cannot start one if it is battle-shocked, OC 0 or “-”, engaged, or it advanced, fell back or started another action this turn; starting one blocks shooting and charging for the turn (and any shooting blocks actions), and moving before it completes fails it. A real trade, so pick your task units before the Shooting phase.

Hold from safety: the Hidden rule (13.09) is the intended way to sit on objectives. A couple of models toed into the area, behind a solid wall for Gone to Ground, force the enemy inside 12″ to 15″ before they can even see you.

The end of the battle: after the last round, and not before. Being tabled does not end it early; players play out their remaining turns, and scoring continues. Under official event scoring, VP cap at 45 primary (up to 15 per round), 45 secondaries (up to 15 per round, 20 per Fixed card) and 10 for a Battle Ready painted army: a perfect 100, and paint is worth real points. Highest total wins; a tie is a draw.

Reading mission cards: a “cumulative” condition scores on top of the normal one, but “or” conditions never stack; a unit “leaves the battlefield” if destroyed, embarked or removed by a rule; an underlined “one” means exactly one; and “up to XVP” is a hard ceiling.

06Command Pointsstratagems

Command Points (CP) are your tactical currency. You gain 1 in every Command phase, including your opponent's turn, and spend them on stratagems: your detachment's own, plus the ten core stratagems every army has (15.02 to 15.12).

The two limits

You cannot use the same stratagem twice in one phase, and you cannot target the same unit with two stratagems in one phase. Command Re-roll is a stratagem: re-rolling a unit's charge locks that unit out of everything else this phase.

1 CPCommand Re-roll

Re-roll one hit, wound, save, damage, advance, hazard, charge or attacks roll. One die only, except charges, which re-roll in full.

1 CPEpic Challenge

When your character unit is selected to fight, one character's melee weapons gain Precision for the phase.

1 CPInsane Bravery

Auto-pass a battle-shock roll, used just before rolling. Once per battle; cannot rescue an already-shocked unit.

1 CPExplosives

An unengaged grenades unit that did not advance picks a visible enemy within 8″: six D6, each 4+ a mortal wound.

1 CPCrushing Impact

A monster or vehicle ending a charge rolls its Toughness in dice: 5+ wounds the enemy (max 6), each 1 wounds itself.

1 CPRapid Ingress

End of the enemy's Movement phase: a reserves unit (not aircraft) ingresses early. Not in round one.

1 CPFire Overwatch

End of the enemy's Movement phase: an unengaged unit snap-shoots one visible target within 24″. Only unmodified 6s hit, no re-rolls.

1 CPSmokescreen

Start of the enemy's Shooting phase: a Smoke unit gains cover, and so does anything screened behind its models.

1 CPHeroic Intervention

End of the enemy's Charge phase: counter-charge a unit that charged, or pay +1 CP to charge anything within 6″ (roll capped at 6).

2 CPCounter-offensive

Just after an enemy unit fights: one of your eligible units gains Fights First and fights next.

Each stratagem states when it can be used, what it targets and what it costs. Counter-offensive is the only core stratagem that costs 2 CP; the rest are 1 CP (Heroic Intervention's wide mode pays +1). Spend CP freely; hoarding them wins nothing.

07Their Turn: Your Windowsthe opponent's turn

You are never just watching. These are the moments in your opponent's turn where you act; missing them is one of the biggest gaps between occasional and regular players. Within any shared timing window the active player resolves their rules first, so as the opposing player you always get the final say (01.03).

08Rules People Get Wrongsettle it before it starts

Every entry here is a genuine table dispute the community keeps having to settle. Rule numbers point at the Core Rules; the long-form explanations live on Rules Insights.

  1. Cover is a worse hit roll, not a better save (13.08). It needs every model in the target unit to qualify, and it is checked per attacking model, so an attacking unit can split into penalised and clean groups.
  2. Roll the charge first, pick targets second (11.02, 11.04). Targets must be within the distance rolled, point to point: a unit 10″ away is not a legal target on a 9, even though the move would reach engagement range.
  3. Charges do not need base contact (11.04). End closer, within 1″ if possible, engaged if possible. Staying off base contact is often correct: based models cannot pile in next phase (12.02).
  4. Command Re-roll locks the unit (15.01). One stratagem per unit per phase, and the re-roll counts: nothing else on that unit this phase.
  5. Invulnerable saves are not optional (05.03). The roll is checked against both saves; you cannot deliberately fail to let a unit die on your terms.
  6. Battle-shock tests at half strength, and recovery is not automatic (08.03). Shocked units keep testing every Command phase, and Insane Bravery cannot be used on them.
  7. Fight eligibility locks at the start of the Fight step (12.04). Killing everything engaged with a unit does not deny its fight; it fights as an overrun fight with a bonus pile-in (12.06). The counterplay is leaving exactly one model alive within 2″.
  8. The active player picks the first Fights First unit (12.04). Charging a Fights First monster is no longer automatically fatal: your chargers have Fights First too, and you activate first.
  9. Modifiers have a fixed order (02.02). Set values first (a 0 or “-” stops everything), then multiply, add, divide, subtract. Melta no longer beats half-damage, and no banner rescues battle-shocked OC.
  10. Arriving from reserves ends your movement for the turn (20.04). An ingress move (Deep Strike included) means no further moves until your next Charge phase, nothing arrives in round one, and reserves still waiting after round three die (20.01).
  11. You can move through enemy engagement range (03.04), you just cannot end there. And coherency now caps a unit inside a 9″ circle (03.03): the conga line is dead.
  12. Hidden breaks on shooting this turn or last (13.09), works in light terrain areas as well as dense (rules commentary), applies per model, and works on turn one. Fire Overwatch counts as shooting.
  13. Overwatch happens at the end of the Movement phase (15.08), not during charges. In the Charge phase the counterplay is Heroic Intervention (15.11).
  14. Disembarking uses the unit's move, and no re-embarking after being set up (18.02). A unit that got out this turn cannot get back in.
  15. Rapid Fire and Melta check half range at the Select Targets step (04.02), against the target unit, not the model you happen to see; resolving other weapons first cannot turn them off.
09Your First Gameor first in a while
  1. Both players build a 1,000 point (Incursion) list in the Army Builder.
  2. Pick one primary mission, one deployment map and one layout from the Missions page. Skip twists and secondaries the first time.
  3. Set up terrain and objectives per the layout, roll off, deploy, roll for the first turn.
  4. Play all five battle rounds with the phase list in front of you. Expect the first game to be slow; by round three the rhythm (Command, Move, Shoot, Charge, Fight) is automatic.
  5. Score primaries at the timing printed on the mission card, and total victory points at the end.
Common first-game slips

Forgetting that both players gain CP each Command phase. Forgetting battle-shock tests for weakened units. Treating cover as a better save instead of a worse hit roll for the shooter. Forgetting to roll the charge before picking targets. And leaving objectives bare because everything went hunting kills.

Back after a break?

The five minutes best spent before the game: re-read chapter 07 (your windows in their turn), chapter 08 (the misplays), and your own detachment rules and stratagems. Most rust shows up as missed windows, not forgotten fundamentals.

Your turn, phase by phase

  • Command: both gain 1 CP; battle-shock rolls; command abilities; mission scoring last.
  • Movement: move or stay; reserves ingress (round two on); embark or disembark; end of phase is the enemy's Overwatch and Rapid Ingress window.
  • Shooting: pick a shooting type per unit; all targets before any dice; check cover, hidden, plunging fire; shooting blocks actions.
  • Charge: eligible within 12″; roll 2D6, then pick targets within the roll; chargers gain Fights First; enemy Heroic Intervention at the end.
  • Fight: all pile-ins (you first), alternating fights (Fights First first, you pick first), then consolidations.
