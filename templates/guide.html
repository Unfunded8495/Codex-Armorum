How to Actually Play

From a blank table to the final score. A walkthrough for your first game of 11th Edition, illustrated at every step.

Jump to the battle round Just tell me what to do first

The Core Rules explain every rule in isolation, but they never tell you what to physically do first, second and third on game day. This guide does. Section numbers in brackets (like 07.02) match the reference numbers used in the Core Rules, so you can always jump there for the fine print.

01What You Needbefore game day

A game of Warhammer 40,000 is two armies fighting over objectives on a terrain-covered table, for a set number of turns. To play, gather:

  • Two armies of Citadel miniatures, one per player, built to an agreed points size (see the next chapter).
  • An army list for each player: which units, which wargear, which enhancements. Build and check yours in the Army Builder.
  • A table with terrain. The mission's deployment map defines the battlefield size and the two deployment zones.
  • Terrain: ruins, containers, woods, crates. More is better than less; a bare table heavily favours shooting armies.
  • Dice (a good handful of D6), a tape measure in inches, and something to mark objectives.
  • A mission, generated before you begin, from a mission deck or the Missions page.

Read a datasheet first

Everything a unit can do lives on its datasheet: its characteristics (Movement, Toughness, Save, Wounds, Objective Control and so on), its weapons, its abilities and its keywords. You will have one per unit in your army. Learn to read one and most of the game falls into place.

Every model you own has its full datasheet in the Weapon Loadouts and catalogue views on this site.

03Setting Up the Tablebefore the first turn

Your mission card lists the exact setup sequence; below is the shape almost every matched-play mission takes. Do all of this before any dice are rolled in anger.

1 · Generate the mission

Before anything is placed, settle on a primary mission (how you score most points), a deployment map (below), a mission layout (where terrain and objectives sit) and, if you both agree, a twist (a battle-wide rule such as Night Fighting). The Missions page holds the whole current matched-play pack with scoring text.

2 · Build the battlefield

Place terrain following the layout, or by agreement (13.01). Each cluster of terrain (or the mat it sits on) defines a terrain area. Terrain comes in three categories (13.02): Exposed (craters, debris: no real cover), Light (barricades, low walls: can give cover, little obstruction) and Dense (buildings, ruins, woods: blocks sight and movement, and matters most). When in doubt, add more dense terrain and leave room around it for big models.

3 · Place objectives

The map shows several objective points. Each should sit on a terrain area; that whole area is the objective, a terrain objective (14.01). A model is within range of it while it is within that area. Standing on objectives is how you win, so mark them clearly.

{{ fig('diagram-controlling-a-terrain-objective.jpg', 1204, 1145, 'Controlling a terrain objective', 'Each player totals the Objective Control of their models in the area; higher total controls it.') }}

4 · Roll off, deploy, begin

  1. Roll off to decide Attacker and Defender (the mission says what each picks).
  2. Declare Battle Formations. Note the things starting off the table: units embarked in transports, units in strategic reserves (20.01) and units using Deep Strike or Infiltrators. Aircraft must start in reserves (23.01).
  3. Deploy your armies, alternating units, each wholly within your own zone.
  4. Resolve Pre-battle Abilities (Scout moves happen here, 24.31).
  5. Determine the first turn and begin. The same player takes the first turn every battle round (07.02).
04The Battle Roundthe heart of the game

The game runs in battle rounds (usually five). In each round the first player takes a full turn, then the second player takes a full turn (07). Every turn moves through five phases in this fixed order:

1Command 2Movement 3Shooting 4Charge 5Fight

Command phase (08)

Both players gain 1 Command Point (yes, both, every turn). Then the active player makes a battle-shock roll for each unit that is at or below half strength, or already battle-shocked (08.03). While a unit is battle-shocked its Objective Control drops to zero (shown as “-”), so it stops holding objectives; it also cannot be targeted by its own stratagems and cannot start or finish actions. A failed unit stays shaken and keeps testing until a battle-shock roll succeeds.

{{ fig('diagram-battle-shock-examples.jpg', 711, 1766, 'Battle-shock examples', 'Which units test, and what failing means.') }}

Movement phase (09)

Move your units one at a time. Each picks a move type: Remain Stationary, a Normal Move (up to its Move characteristic), an Advance (Move + D6, but usually no shooting or charging after), or a Fall Back to escape combat at a cost. Units end moves in coherency and cannot move within engagement range (2″ horizontally, 5″ vertically) of the enemy. Reserves and Deep Strike units also arrive now.

{{ fig('diagram-moving-in-a-straight-line.jpg', 713, 715, 'Moving in a straight line', 'Measure every part of the model as it moves.') }} {{ fig('diagram-rotating.jpg', 1207, 717, 'Rotating a model', 'Turning on the spot still counts as movement.') }} {{ fig('diagram-coherency.jpg', 1205, 873, 'Unit coherency', 'Models must stay within 2 inches of the unit.') }} {{ fig('diagram-engagement.jpg', 1209, 872, 'Engagement range', 'The 2 inch / 5 inch bubble around every model.') }}

Shooting phase (10)

Pick each eligible unit in turn: select the weapons, select target units that are visible and in range, then resolve the attacks (below). A model is a valid target only if it can be seen, so line of sight and cover matter enormously.

{{ fig('diagram-model-visible.jpg', 960, 424, 'Model visible', 'Any part seen means the model is visible.') }} {{ fig('diagram-model-fully-visible.jpg', 956, 425, 'Model fully visible', 'The whole model can be seen.') }} {{ fig('diagram-unit-visible.jpg', 958, 421, 'Unit visible', 'At least one model in the unit is visible.') }} {{ fig('diagram-unit-fully-visible.jpg', 957, 418, 'Unit fully visible', 'Every model in the unit is visible.') }} {{ fig('diagram-benefit-of-cover.jpg', 589, 1144, 'Benefit of cover', 'Cover worsens the shooter\'s BS by 1; it does not improve the save.') }} {{ fig('diagram-hidden-and-obscuring.jpg', 593, 1143, 'Hidden and obscuring', 'Dense terrain blocks sight; a hidden unit is only seen within 15 inches.') }}

Terrain changes who can be hit. In 11th Edition the benefit of cover does not improve a save; instead it worsens the shooter's Ballistic Skill by 1 (a harder hit roll), and it applies to Infantry, Beasts or Swarm units in a terrain area or units screened by intervening terrain (13.08). A hidden Infantry, Beasts or Swarm unit sitting in dense terrain that has not shot this turn or last can only be targeted from within 15″ (13.09).

The attack sequence (05): how every attack resolves

Shooting and fighting both use the same four steps. Roll each step's dice together:

  1. Hit rolls: one D6 per attack. Meet or beat the weapon's BS (shooting) or WS (melee). An unmodified 1 always misses; an unmodified 6 is a Critical Hit.
  2. Wound rolls: one D6 per hit, comparing Strength to Toughness (see the table in Quick Reference). Unmodified 1 fails, unmodified 6 is a Critical Wound.
  3. Save rolls: the defender rolls one D6 per wound, trying to beat the weapon's AP-modified save, or an invulnerable save (which ignores AP).
  4. Inflict damage: each failed save costs a model the weapon's Damage. At 0 wounds a model is destroyed. Excess damage does not carry to the next model.

Charge phase (11)

To fight in melee a unit must charge, and the order matters. First pick an eligible unit: within 12″ of an enemy, not already engaged, and it has not advanced or fallen back this turn. Then roll 2D6 for the maximum distance (11.02). Only after the roll do you choose targets: enemy units within 12″ and within the distance you rolled (11.04). The charge succeeds only if the unit can end engaged with every target it chose, without touching anything it did not. Fall short and the unit does not move at all. Charging pays off: chargers strike with Fights First.

{{ fig('diagram-making-a-charge-move.jpg', 1201, 974, 'Making a charge move', 'Roll 2D6 first, then choose targets within the distance rolled.') }}

Fight phase (12)

Both players act here, in three steps: Pile In (a 3″ move to tighten combat), Fight (units fight one at a time, Fights First units before the rest, players alternating) and Consolidate (a final 3″ move). Fighting uses the same attack sequence as shooting. You must fight with every unit that can.

05Scoring & Winningvictory points

Kills alone do not win games; victory points do, and most come from holding objectives.

Controlling an objective (14.02): at the end of each phase and turn, each player adds up the Objective Control (OC) of all their models within the objective's terrain area. Highest total controls it; a tie means nobody holds it unless it is secured (14.03).

Primary mission: your card says when and how you score, usually for holding objectives at the end of your turn or the battle round. The End of Turn and End of Battle Round steps exist so you remember to check (07.03).

Secondary missions score alongside the primary, drawn as the game goes on. Actions (16) are battlefield tasks some missions need; a unit that starts one cannot shoot or declare a charge for the rest of the turn, and battle-shocked or OC-zero units cannot start them at all, so they are a real trade.

The battle ends after the last round (or if an army is wiped out). Highest total victory points wins.

06Command Pointsstratagems

Command Points (CP) are your tactical currency. You gain 1 in every Command phase, including your opponent's turn, and spend them on stratagems: your detachment's own, plus the ten core stratagems every army has (15.02 to 15.12).

1 CPCommand Re-roll

Re-roll one hit, wound, save, damage, advance, hazard, charge or attacks roll.

1 CPEpic Challenge

Give a character's melee weapons Precision to pick out enemy characters.

1 CPInsane Bravery

Auto-pass a battle-shock test. Once per battle.

1 CPExplosives

A grenades or explosives unit throws for mortal wounds instead of shooting.

1 CPCrushing Impact

A monster or vehicle that finishes a charge deals mortal wounds.

1 CPRapid Ingress

Bring a reserves unit onto the table early, at the end of the enemy's move.

1 CPFire Overwatch

An unengaged unit shoots in the enemy's turn, but hits only on unmodified 6s.

1 CPSmokescreen

A Smoke unit gains the benefit of cover in your opponent's Shooting phase.

1 CPHeroic Intervention

An unengaged unit counter-charges in the enemy's Charge phase (2 CP for wider reach).

2 CPCounter-offensive

An eligible unit fights next, out of the normal sequence.

Each stratagem states when it can be used, what it targets and what it costs. Counter-offensive is the only core stratagem that costs 2 CP; the rest are 1 CP. Spend CP freely; hoarding them wins nothing.

07Your First Gamea suggested script
  1. Both players build a 1,000 point (Incursion) list in the Army Builder.
  2. Pick one primary mission, one deployment map and one layout from the Missions page. Skip twists and secondaries the first time.
  3. Set up terrain and objectives per the layout, roll off, deploy, roll for the first turn.
  4. Play all five battle rounds with the phase list in front of you. Expect the first game to be slow; by round three the rhythm (Command, Move, Shoot, Charge, Fight) is automatic.
  5. Score primaries at the timing printed on the mission card, and total victory points at the end.
Common first-game slips

Forgetting that both players gain CP each Command phase. Forgetting battle-shock tests for weakened units. Treating cover as a better save instead of a worse hit roll for the shooter. Forgetting to roll the charge before picking targets. And leaving objectives bare because everything went hunting kills.
